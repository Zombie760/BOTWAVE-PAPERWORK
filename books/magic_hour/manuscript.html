Chapter 1

June 1979

The largest single sports transaction in American history to that point was, like most things in Los Angeles, a real-estate deal.

It is the spring of 1979. A man named Jerry Buss — born Gerald Hatten Buss in Salt Lake City, raised by a single mother in Kemmerer, Wyoming, holder of a Ph.D. in physical chemistry from USC, age forty-six, divorced, tan, smoker, holder of a real-estate fortune accumulated through a partnership called Mariani-Buss Associates that started with starting capital under ten thousand dollars in 1959 and ended, twenty years later, with a portfolio of apartment buildings worth roughly three hundred and fifty million dollars — sits in a meeting in a high office in Century City, and across from him sits a Canadian named Jack Kent Cooke. Cooke is sixty-six. Cooke is the owner of the Los Angeles Lakers, the Los Angeles Kings, the Forum in Inglewood where both teams play their home games, and a sprawling horse property in the Sierra foothills. Cooke wishes to sell all of these things.

He wishes to sell them because his first wife, after more than four decades of marriage, has divorced him, and the California courts, in what is at the time the largest divorce settlement in American history, have ordered him to pay her forty-one million dollars. Cooke does not have forty-one million dollars in cash. He has assets. The Lakers and the Kings and the Forum are assets. So is the ranch.

Buss wants the Lakers.

Buss does not, particularly, want the Kings, who are a hockey team, and Buss does not understand hockey. Buss does not particularly want the ranch, which is in the Sierra foothills, and Buss is a Pacific Coast man with no use for foothills. What Buss wants — what Buss has wanted for years, ever since he first sat in the Forum’s loge seats and watched Wilt Chamberlain block a shot and decided that he would, someday, in some way, own the building this happened in, is a basketball team in Los Angeles.

The deal Cooke offers him is a package deal. Lakers, Kings, Forum, ranch. Sixty-seven and a half million dollars. All or nothing.

Buss takes it.

He takes it the way a man takes a hand of cards he has been waiting on for a decade. He takes it the way a chemist solves an equation. He takes it the way the city of Los Angeles, in 1979, takes most things, quickly, with a smile, and on credit. Because Buss does not have sixty-seven and a half million dollars either. What Buss has is a partnership stake in a real-estate venture that includes, among other holdings, a piece of the Chrysler Building in midtown Manhattan. The financing of the largest sports transaction in American history is therefore arranged as a swap. Cooke takes the Chrysler Building. Buss takes the Lakers. The Forum and the Kings and the ranch and the rest of the package change hands accordingly. The IRS counts the swap as a like-kind exchange and the capital gains are deferred. Everyone signs. It is, in the technical sense, a real-estate transaction. In every other sense, it is the founding of Showtime.

It is May. Then it is June. Then it is closed.

There is a photograph somewhere in the LA Times morgue of Buss, the day the deal goes public, smiling on the floor of an empty Forum, before a game, in a leather jacket with the collar up, looking less like a sports owner than like the guy a casting agent would later choose to play one. The smile is the smile of a man who understands what he has just bought.

He has bought the building.

He has bought the team that plays in the building.

He has bought the second team that plays in the building.

He has bought, although it will take him about six months to figure out how, the city.

Showtime, the era, starts here. Not with Magic Johnson, who has not yet been drafted — he will be selected first overall by the Lakers on June 25, 1979, three weeks or so after the franchise sale closes — and not with Pat Riley, who is at this moment a thirty-four-year-old retired guard doing color commentary on Lakers broadcasts and has no idea he will ever coach anything, and not with the Forum Club, which is at this moment a serviceable arena bar that closes when the games end. Showtime starts with Buss in Cooke’s office, signing.

What Buss understands, on the day he signs, that no one else in the National Basketball Association understands, is that the ticket to the game is not the only revenue stream associated with the building. The bar is a revenue stream. The parking is a revenue stream. The concessions are a revenue stream. The concert tour that comes through the Forum on the off nights is a revenue stream. The cheerleaders are a revenue stream. (He will name them the Laker Girls, in the autumn of 1979, and over the next two decades nearly every NBA team will copy the format.) The celebrity guest list is a revenue stream — not in the direct sense, because the celebrities are not paying, but in the second-order sense, because the photograph of Jack Nicholson in the front row sells more season tickets than any advertising budget the league has ever conceived. The whole apparatus is a revenue stream. The basketball, in this view, is the centerpiece, but the centerpiece is not the whole table.

This was, in 1979, a heretical understanding of how a sports franchise made money. The other twenty-one franchises in the NBA at the time — the league had twenty-two teams in the 1979–80 season, the Mavericks not yet expanded into Dallas, did not run nightclubs. They did not run cheerleader brands. They did not curate celebrity courtside seating as a form of off-balance-sheet marketing. They sold tickets, sold a few hot dogs, sold the local TV rights to whichever station bid highest, and went home. The financial structure of the NBA in 1979 was so weak that the league’s championship game, the championship game, Game 7 if it happened, had been tape-delayed by CBS, played at eleven-thirty at night after the local news, on the theory that no one in primetime America wanted to watch professional basketball. Buss was buying into a league whose ratings were thinner than reruns of M*A*S*H.

He was not, however, buying a league.

He was buying a building, in a city, that had already invented the entire vocabulary of celebrity. All he had to do was bring the celebrities to the building, put the building on television, and let the city sell itself to itself, on his behalf, every Tuesday and Thursday night for the next forty years.

The first thing he did, after signing, was hire a publicity director.

The second thing he did was call a man named Earvin Johnson, Jr., a six-foot-nine point guard from Lansing, Michigan, who had won the NCAA championship that March against Larry Bird and Indiana State, and tell him that Los Angeles was about to become the most fun city on the planet and that he, Earvin, was going to be the reason.

Magic, twenty years old, called him Dr. Buss.

Showtime had begun.

The thing to understand about Buss — and the thing the league did not understand about him for at least three years, by which point it was too late, is that he was not a sports guy.

He was a Los Angeles guy.

The distinction matters. Sports guys, in 1979, were the people who had inherited or grown into ownership of franchises by working in the sport itself. They were former players, former general managers, former agents. Their reference set was the league. They asked the league how to do things and they got the league’s answer. Buss was not in the league’s reference set. He was in the city’s. He went to clubs on Sunset. He owned the Pickfair mansion, eventually, the same house Mary Pickford and Douglas Fairbanks had built — bought it in 1980 from Pia Zadora’s husband for some millions and lived there with a rotating constellation of much-younger girlfriends and a parade of guests who were, depending on the night, retired actors, working actors, real-estate developers, hockey players, basketball players, and members of the rock-music industry. His Rolodex was not the league’s. His Rolodex was the city’s. And so when he sat down, in late 1979, to figure out what to do with his new building, his reference points were not the Boston Garden or Madison Square Garden or the Cow Palace.

His reference points were the Whisky a Go Go. The Roxy. The Troubadour.

He was building a nightclub that happened to host professional basketball.

This sounds, in 2026, almost banal. Of course an NBA arena is a nightclub. Of course the music is loud and the cheerleaders dance and the celebrities sit courtside and the league markets the entire experience as a multi-modal entertainment product. That is the league. But in 1979, that was not the league. That was Buss. And the reason it became the league is that the building Buss built, the building, and the team in it, and the era they generated together, was so much more profitable, and so much more famous, and so much more fun, than every other building in the league, that within a decade, every other building in the league had to copy it or die.

Showtime is what we call it.

Buss called it real estate.

He was, as it turned out, both right.

Magic Johnson signed his rookie contract on July 26, 1979. Five years, five hundred thousand dollars per year, plus signing bonus. The number is laughable now, the way mortgage rates from the 1970s are laughable now. At the time, it was the largest rookie contract in the history of professional basketball. Buss paid it out of the building’s revenue. The building, in the autumn of 1979, was just starting to figure out what it could be.

What it was about to become, that autumn, was the loudest, brightest, most-televised arena in the western United States, host to a six-nine point guard who had never met a no-look pass he could not throw, paired with a thirty-two-year-old seven-foot-two captain named Lew Alcindor who had legally changed his name in 1971 to Kareem Abdul-Jabbar and who possessed a single, unblockable, hook-shot weapon that the league would spend the next decade trying and failing to defend against.

Together they were going to win five championships in nine seasons.

Together they were going to invent the modern NBA.

Together they were going to make Buss, the man who in May of 1979 had traded a piece of the Chrysler Building for the rights to host them, one of the wealthiest team owners in American sports.

But all of that comes later.

This is June. In June, Buss signs.

Chapter 2

The Hug

October 12, 1979. San Diego Sports Arena. A Friday night, the opening game of the NBA season for both teams on the floor, the Los Angeles Lakers in gold, the San Diego Clippers in red and white, the score tied at one hundred and one with eight seconds left on the clock, the ball in the hands of Kareem Abdul-Jabbar at the right elbow. Kareem is thirty-two years old. He has been in the league for eleven years. He has won one championship, with Milwaukee in 1971, back when he was still legally named Lew Alcindor and had not yet made the pilgrimage to Islam or the pilgrimage to Los Angeles. He is, on this night, the best center in basketball and arguably the best low-post player the sport has yet produced, and he possesses a single move — the skyhook, released from roughly seven feet eight inches in the air, off the fingertips of his fully extended right arm — which nobody, in the history of organized basketball up to that point, has figured out how to block.

He catches the pass at the right elbow.

He pivots toward the baseline.

He raises his right arm.

And over the head of the Clippers’ six-foot-eleven center, a man named Bill Walton who is incidentally the only other center in the league arguably capable of thinking his way through a skyhook, the ball floats on a parabola that is for all practical purposes indefensible, traveling the ten feet from Kareem’s fingertips to the rim in roughly a second and a quarter, and at the end of that second and a quarter it enters the basket from directly above. Lakers one hundred and three, Clippers one hundred and one. Game over.

This is when it happens.

A twenty-year-old rookie named Earvin Johnson Jr., six-foot-nine, Lansing-born, grinning, who has been running the offense at point guard for the entire fourth quarter, sprints across the court toward Kareem with both arms in the air. He does not stop at Kareem. He leaps onto Kareem. He gets both arms around Kareem’s shoulders, both feet off the ground, and he hangs there like a boy swinging on a rail.

Kareem is not a hugger.

Kareem does not, in 1979, embrace men he has known for a few months in visible public displays of emotion, particularly not in game one of eighty-two when the season still has eighty-one games to settle. Kareem is reserved. Kareem is careful. Kareem is wary of the press and wary of the crowd and wary of a league that has, for the previous decade, treated his conversion to Islam as a provocation and his opinions as a nuisance. He plays basketball like a chess grandmaster with a sore back. He does not, in 1979, hug.

A twenty-year-old rookie is hanging off his neck.

The look on Kareem’s face, in the photographs that ran in the next morning’s papers, is a look somewhere between confusion and resignation. He is, visibly, trying to work out whether this is a breach of decorum he needs to correct or a phenomenon he needs to accept. The rookie is grinning, saying something to him. Kareem, in later recollections of the moment, said he laughed.

And that was when Kareem Abdul-Jabbar, thirty-two years old, the best center in basketball, understood that the next several years of his career were going to be different than anything he had played through before.

To understand why the hug was a moment, and not just a celebration, you have to understand what Kareem had been for the previous four years with the Lakers.

He had been lonely.

The Lakers had acquired him from Milwaukee on June 16, 1975, at his own request — he wanted a bigger city, more cultural life, and a team that was not Milwaukee in February — and they had paid for him with four players and a chunk of the draft. He had arrived in Los Angeles as the most famous basketball player alive and he had played four seasons for the Lakers in which his teams posted winning records but failed to reach the Finals in any of them. The rosters around him were competent without being thrilling. The style of play was methodical. The Forum crowd, even in those years, was a good crowd, not a great one. Kareem scored his thirty points a night. He grabbed his thirteen rebounds. He went home to his books and his jazz records, and he waited, with an increasingly visible fatigue, for the league to give him something to work with.

In June of 1979, the league gave him Magic.

The draft had been held on June 25. Jerry Buss had signed the paperwork on the franchise less than a month before. The draft was Buss’s first act as an owner. He had inherited the first overall pick from the previous season’s ownership, the Lakers held it via a prior trade with the last-place New Orleans Jazz, and he had used the pick on the most charismatic college basketball player in the country, a six-foot-nine point guard (point guard, at six-nine, a position that did not exist at that height until Magic invented it) from Michigan State, who had beaten Larry Bird of Indiana State in the NCAA championship game that March in front of a television audience that set the college-basketball rating record that would stand for decades. Magic was twenty. Magic signed his rookie contract on July 26 for a reported half-million dollars a year across five years, the richest rookie deal in NBA history up to that point. Magic arrived in Los Angeles at the end of the summer and reported to training camp in Palm Desert. Kareem sized him up.

What Kareem saw was a kid who smiled all the time.

Kareem did not, at first, trust the smile.

By the end of October, he would.

The Lakers finished the 1979–80 regular season sixty and twenty-two, which was the best record in the Western Conference and the second-best in the league behind a Celtics team that had just finished Larry Bird’s rookie year in Boston. They had, during the season, two head coaches — Jack McKinney, Buss’s first hire and the man who had designed the run-and-gun offense that would come to be known as Showtime, was injured in a bicycle accident in November and had to resign, and his assistant Paul Westhead was elevated mid-season — and a string of injuries, and, by March, the first real championship run the franchise had mounted since the Wilt Chamberlain years. They came out of the West. They faced Philadelphia in the Finals.

What happened next is the next chapter.

But the season started on a Friday night in San Diego, in the fall of 1979, with a skyhook over Bill Walton’s outstretched arm and a rookie hanging off the captain’s neck, and a photograph that would run in every Los Angeles paper the next morning.

The photograph was the thesis.

The thesis was that one of them was the blueprint and the other was the architect. Kareem was the blueprint: thirty points a night, thirteen rebounds, a skyhook nobody could block, the game’s most reliable scoring weapon for a full decade already, the immutable object in a league that was otherwise in constant motion. Magic was the architect: the connector, the passer, the broadcaster of the entire team’s joy outward to the eighteen thousand paying customers in the arena and the millions of television viewers who were about to discover that the NBA, of all things, was fun again.

Put the blueprint and the architect in the same building for nine years, and you got Showtime.

Put them together for one Friday night in San Diego, and you got the hug.

Chapter 3

Magic Plays Center

The flight from Los Angeles to Philadelphia on the afternoon of May fifteenth, 1980, carried a basketball team with one fewer man on it than it had the day before.

Kareem Abdul-Jabbar was back at the Forum, in a trainer’s room, with his left ankle packed in ice. He had hurt the ankle in the third quarter of Game Five, which the Lakers had won in Los Angeles the night before, and he had come back into the game in the fourth quarter, on the injured ankle, and scored forty points, which was the kind of thing he had been doing in a Lakers uniform for five years, and which the medical staff had informed him, after the game, he would not be doing again for at least a week. He would not travel to Philadelphia. He would not play in Game Six. If the Lakers lost in Philadelphia, they would come home and try to win Game Seven at the Forum on Kareem’s ankle. If they won in Philadelphia, they would come home champions.

It was very much an if.

The team that boarded the plane at LAX on the fifteenth was a team without its captain, without its point guard playing his natural position, and without, in the view of most observers, a credible chance against a Philadelphia 76ers team that was at home, healthy, coached by Billy Cunningham, with Julius Erving at the three, Darryl Dawkins at the five, and a young Maurice Cheeks running the point. The Sixers were not, as the cliché goes, intimidated. The Sixers were relieved. They had been playing against Kareem for five games. They were going to be playing against, they assumed, a six-foot-eleven backup named Jim Chones at the five on the visitors’ side of the floor. Whatever happened, it would not be Kareem.

What the Sixers had not yet accounted for was the rookie point guard, age twenty, who sat down in the first-class section of the team charter, looked around at his teammates, looked out the window at the California coastline dropping away, and walked forward down the aisle to the first row, first seat on the left, Kareem’s seat, and sat down in it.

What is on the record is that Magic Johnson, rookie, sat in Kareem’s seat on the flight. What is also on the record is that when the Lakers took the floor in the Spectrum on the evening of May sixteenth, in their gold road jerseys under the Philadelphia lights, Magic Johnson, six feet nine inches tall and twenty years old, walked to the center circle, faced Caldwell Jones of the 76ers, who was six feet eleven inches tall, and jumped center for the opening tip.

It was the first time, and so far the only time, in the history of the NBA Finals that a point guard had jumped center.

Magic won the tip.

The box score for Game Six of the 1980 NBA Finals has become, in the decades since the game was played, one of the iconic artifacts in American professional basketball history. Magic played all five positions on the floor across forty-seven minutes of game time. He finished with forty-two points, fifteen rebounds, and seven assists. The Lakers won, one hundred twenty-three to one hundred seven, to clinch the series four games to two. Magic was named NBA Finals Most Valuable Player. He was the only rookie in NBA history, then or since, to win the award. He was twenty years old.

The CBS broadcast, in those days before the NBA Finals were treated by the American television industry as a tier-one property, was not even televised live in most of the country. The network had decided earlier that spring that the Lakers–Sixers series was not a big enough prime-time draw, and had scheduled the Finals for tape delay, to air after the local eleven-o’clock news. In Los Angeles, fans who wanted to watch Game Six live sat in a darkened living room at six in the evening and listened on the radio to Chick Hearn, who called the game from a courtside seat at the Spectrum, with the kind of high-speed play-by-play cadence — stuffed with his own invented vocabulary, slam dunk and no harm, no foul and airball and the mustard’s off the hot dog — that he had been perfecting since the Lakers were still playing their home games at the Sports Arena downtown, before the Forum was built, before Kareem was traded in from Milwaukee, before Buss had dreamt of owning the building.

Chick was the voice of the broadcast.

Magic was the voice of the floor.

By the end of the third quarter, the Spectrum crowd had stopped booing.

By the end of the fourth quarter, the Spectrum crowd was standing.

When the buzzer went, and the Lakers mobbed Magic at mid-court, the CBS cameras, which had been sent out to tape the game for the next night’s delayed broadcast, captured a shot that would run in every American newspaper the next morning: Magic, sweat-drenched, one sneaker untied, with his arm around Jamaal Wilkes on one side and Michael Cooper on the other, looking into the nearest camera and smiling so widely that the smile is what the photograph is about.

Kareem, watching the game from Los Angeles on his sprained ankle, would later describe the night as the one he understood Magic was going to change the franchise.

Jerry Buss, eleven months into his ownership of the franchise, watched his twenty-year-old rookie play five positions and beat Julius Erving’s Philadelphia 76ers by sixteen points, in the franchise’s first championship of his ownership.

On the plane home, the Lakers drank champagne, spilled champagne, played cards, and replayed the fourth quarter in fragments, as ballplayers always do on the ride home from a title.

By the time the charter landed at LAX, the morning papers were already printed.

Every front page carried the same photograph.

Kareem, the next day, watched the replay from his Forum recovery room, and when the final buzzer sounded on the tape, he said, to the trainer, and to nobody else, that the rookie was going to be fine.

That was always going to be the shape of the thing.

The team would provide the basketball.

The city would provide the word.

The Lakers were champions.

The rookie was a Finals MVP.

The owner had eleven months of experience.

The era had started.

Chapter 4

The Forum Club

Inside the building, on the south side, on the same level as the courtside seats but accessible only through a narrow corridor behind a velvet rope manned by a bouncer in a dinner jacket, was a private bar.

The bar was called the Forum Club.

The Forum Club had been built into the original architecture of the Forum when the building went up in Inglewood in 1967, but for the first twelve years of its existence — under the ownership of Jack Kent Cooke, who did not drink and did not see the point — it had operated as a small, dim, prime-rib-and-iceberg-lettuce restaurant for season-ticket holders, with a bar at the end and a piano in the corner and the kind of clientele that left at halftime to make their dinner reservations in Beverly Hills. By the close of the 1979 season, when Buss took ownership of the building, the Forum Club was generating roughly the same amount of revenue per square foot as the popcorn vendors.

Buss looked at the Forum Club and saw a different building.

He saw, in the small, dim restaurant, the foundational unit of an entirely new business model for professional sports in the United States, which was to monetize not only the game on the floor but the social ritual that surrounded the game on the floor, on the theory that what the wealthy season-ticket holder was actually paying for was not the basketball but the access to the room where the basketball was being watched. He saw the Forum Club not as a restaurant but as the implicit nightclub the franchise had been failing to build. He saw it as a private membership room in which the celebrity sat next to the surgeon who sat next to the recording-industry lawyer who sat next to the developer who sat next to the divorce lawyer who, in the months and years that followed, would all become season-ticket renewers in perpetuity for the simple reason that membership in the room was, after a while, more valuable than any sum of money the team could have charged for it.

The first thing Buss did was extend the hours.

The second thing Buss did was hire a manager from one of the nightclubs on Sunset.

The third thing Buss did was put a band on stage on Tuesday and Thursday nights.

For the next twelve years, the Lakers were at the center of every party in town.

The Forum Club, by the spring of 1980, was the most desirable single room in the city of Los Angeles.

Documented Forum Club regulars across the era — Jack Nicholson, Dyan Cannon, and a wide cast of working actors, recording-industry guests, and other Los Angeles figures who occupied the front rows of the seating bowl — were photographed, named in the Los Angeles Times society columns of the period, and discussed in published Forum Club histories.

Buss himself was the host. He worked the room. He ran it, more or less continuously, until the late 1990s.

What the Forum Club did to the basketball, sometimes, was not visible in the box score.

What the Forum Club did was give the Lakers the cultural equivalent of a home-field advantage that was generally unavailable in professional sports up to that point. Visiting teams who came to the Forum did not just play in front of an opposing crowd, in an opposing arena, on an opposing piece of hardwood. They played, on the road, and knew that no matter how the game went, after it was over, the home team was going back to a private nightclub to drink and laugh and dance with movie stars, and they, the visiting team, were going back to a Marriott on Century Boulevard with a continental breakfast voucher tucked into a folder. The visiting team’s shooters knew it. The visiting team’s coaches knew it. The visiting team’s young players, especially, knew it. The Lakers were, over time, the cool kids’ team, in a league that had not previously had a cool kids’ team because the league had not previously had a building like the Forum or an owner like Jerry Buss.

The Celtics had Boston Garden, which was, in the early 1980s, a charmingly run-down basketball cathedral that smelled like sweat and old hot dogs and where the parquet floor had dead spots that Larry Bird could navigate by memory and visiting teams could not.

The Lakers had the Forum, which had a nightclub.

When the two teams met in the Finals, the Boston press routinely framed the Lakers as the embodiment of the Los Angeles that Boston could neither produce nor defeat — the warm-weather, palm-tree, party-after-the-game, glossier-on-the-magazine-cover kind of city that Boston, in its self-image, openly considered beneath itself.

That was the deal.

The Forum had the nightclub.

Boston had the parquet.

In the 1980s, more often than not, Boston won.

That, too, was the deal. The team that had the parquet had a tradition of winning that no nightclub could compete with. The team that had the nightclub had a tradition of being seen that no parquet could compete with.

It would take Pat Riley, eventually, to fold the tradition of being seen into the tradition of winning, and produce the only basketball franchise in the history of the league that managed both at once.

But that is the next chapter.

What needs to be said, before we leave the Forum Club, is that the Forum Club was — for all the celebrity, for all the money, for all the leather banquettes and the cocaine in the bathrooms in years that the Lakers’ coaches and Lakers’ management would have preferred didn’t happen — the public face of a private theory of how a sports franchise creates value.

The theory, by Buss’s own subsequent description in interviews, was simple:

The fans were not paying for the basketball.

They were paying for the night.

The basketball was a piece of the night, but it was not the whole night. The whole night included the drive over, the ramp into the parking structure, the walk through the corridor with the gold-and-purple banners overhead, the seat-finding, the Laker Girls during the first timeout, the in-game music, the celebrity-spotting, the food, the score running across the bottom of the scoreboard, the postgame walk back to the car if you were not in the Forum Club, and the postgame walk to the velvet rope if you were. The basketball was thirty-six minutes of game clock spread across the better part of three hours of evening. The basketball was the occasion for the night, in the same way that a four-star restaurant is the occasion for the date.

What you sold a ticket-holder, Buss said, was the night.

Get the night right, and the ticket-holder would buy a ticket for thirty straight years.

Get it wrong, and the basketball would not save you.

He had, in 1985, been getting the night right for six years.

He would get it right for another seventeen.

The franchise he handed off to his children, in the autumn of 2008, was, in the way that a building is the stamped expression of an architect’s logic, the executed version of an idea he had drawn out, in fountain pen, on the back of a cocktail napkin in the Forum Club some time during the spring of 1980.

The idea was the night.

The Forum Club was the night.

That was Showtime, before anyone called it Showtime.

The team would provide the basketball.

The room would provide the rest.

Chapter 5

Riley

The single most consequential personnel decision Jerry Buss ever made was made on the morning of November 19, 1981, in his office on the fifth floor of the Forum, after a phone conversation with general manager Bill Sharman and the Lakers’ new head coach, who had been on the job for fewer than seventy-two hours, neither of which men had wanted the job in the first place, and one of whom would, in the eleven seasons that followed the conversation, become the most famous and the most stylistically influential head coach in the history of professional basketball.

His name was Patrick James Riley.

He was thirty-six years old.

He had not, two weeks before the conversation, expected to be a head coach for at least another ten years.

He had been, until two weeks before the conversation, a color commentator on the Lakers’ broadcast, sitting next to Chick Hearn in the booth at the Forum.

Then Magic Johnson had a press conference.

The press conference happened on the night of November 18, 1981, in a hallway outside the visitors’ locker room in Salt Lake City, where the Lakers had just lost to the Utah Jazz one hundred and thirteen to one hundred and ten, dropping their record on the season to seven and four. Magic, then twenty-two years old, in his third NBA season, told the small group of beat reporters who had followed him out of the locker room that he was unhappy. He was unhappy, he said, with the offense. The offense, run by head coach Paul Westhead, who had taken the job two years earlier when McKinney was injured, had been redesigned over the summer to slow down — to feature more half-court sets, more screening for Kareem, less of the fast-break pace at which Magic was the best player in the league. Magic did not, in so many words, demand that Westhead be fired. He said only that he was not happy, that he was not having fun, that he wanted out, possibly via trade. The reporters asked him to repeat himself for the tape. He repeated himself. The next morning, the line ran on the wires, in the newspapers, on the radio drive-time shows, in every pre-game show going into the next day’s games — Magic wants out.

Buss, who had been in Las Vegas at one of his other businesses, flew back to Los Angeles overnight.

He landed at five in the morning at LAX.

He drove directly to the Forum.

He fired Westhead at eleven o’clock that morning, in his office, with Sharman in the room, with the press waiting in the hallway outside the office, with Westhead, in a corduroy jacket, behaving with as much dignity as the circumstance allowed.

In the days that followed, Buss, Sharman, and Jerry West (then in a quasi-advisory role with the franchise after stepping back from active management) worked through the head-coaching question. The arrangement that emerged — and which has been retold in slightly different versions by each of the three principals across the decades since — paired West, briefly and reluctantly, in some kind of co-head capacity with Riley, who was promoted from his assistant-coaching role into the head chair. Within a few games, West had stepped back out, and Riley was the head coach in his own right.

He did not blow it.

He coached the Lakers for nine seasons, from November of 1981 through the spring of 1990. He won four championships in that span — 1982, 1985, 1987, 1988. He went to the Finals seven times in nine years, which is a number nobody on the Eastern Conference side of the league produced even in the heyday of Bird’s Boston, and which has been matched, in the four decades since, only by the Riley-coached Knicks of the 1990s and the Bulls of the second-three-peat. He coached, in those nine years, three Hall-of-Famers in their primes (Magic, Kareem, Worthy) and three more who would later be enshrined (Cooper, McAdoo, Wilkes). He coached against Bird, against Erving, against the Pistons under Chuck Daly, against Don Nelson’s Bucks. He won fifty-eight, sixty-two, sixty-three, fifty-four, fifty-eight, sixty-five, sixty-two, fifty-seven, and sixty-three games in those nine seasons, never under fifty-four, for a total regular-season record of five hundred and thirty-three wins against one hundred and ninety-four losses, which is a winning percentage of seventy-three percent across a near-decade in the most demanding professional basketball league on the planet.

He coached, in short, like a man who had something to prove.

He had, in short, something to prove.

Patrick James Riley had been born in Rome, New York, on March 20, 1945, the youngest of six children in an Irish-American baseball family. His father had played professional baseball in the Cleveland Indians’ minor-league system. His childhood, Riley later wrote, had been organized entirely around competitive sports played in a small upstate New York town where the streetlights came on at six. He had grown up tough. He had played football and basketball at Linton High School in Schenectady. He had gone to Kentucky on a basketball scholarship, played four seasons under Adolph Rupp, captained the Wildcats team that lost to Don Haskins’s Texas Western team, five Black starters against Rupp’s all-white Kentucky, in the 1966 NCAA championship game, in what is, on many lists, the most consequential single basketball game of the twentieth century. He had been picked seventh overall by the San Diego Rockets in the 1967 NBA draft. He had played nine professional seasons, finished one of them with the Lakers and helped them win their 1972 championship, retired in 1976 at age thirty-one, and gone into broadcasting because he had no idea what else to do with himself.

He was, in 1981, a man who had been very good at every level of basketball without ever being the best player on a roster.

He had also been, in his playing days, the kind of bench player who watched the head coach as carefully as the head coach watched the lineup.

He was, in the strict sense, an apprentice waiting for a chair.

When Buss handed him the chair, on November 19, 1981, the apprenticeship ended and the persona began.

The persona was a coach in Armani.

The persona was a coach with the haircut every NBA assistant for the next thirty years would copy.

The persona was a coach whose pre-game speeches in the Forum locker room — recorded, by the late 1980s, on the assumption that they were going to enter the public record sooner or later — had the rhythmic, alliterative, almost evangelical quality of a Sunday-morning sermon at a black Baptist church, delivered by a white Catholic from upstate New York to a roster that was about half Black and about half Italian-Catholic and about a hundred percent listening.

The persona was a coach who had figured out, somewhere in his second or third year on the job, that what the modern professional athlete needed was not just X’s and O’s but an idea — a phrase to repeat in his head during the fourth quarter, a tagline for the season, a slogan that would survive the boring training-camp afternoons and become, by April, the team’s actual reason for being on the floor.

The 1985 phrase was the Career Best Effort.

The 1986 phrase was the Disease of Me.

The 1987 phrase was the Guarantee, which Riley would offer to the press of his own volition, in the moments after winning the 1987 championship, that the Lakers would repeat as champions in 1988 — a phrase that no head coach in the history of the league had ever offered, that put unprecedented pressure on the players who would have to deliver on it, and that, against every expectation of the league’s other twenty-five teams, the players actually delivered on.

The 1988 phrase was we are champions of the world.

Each phrase was Riley’s. Each phrase entered the player vocabulary by midseason. Each phrase showed up, for the next thirty years, in the books of every coaching imitator who tried to figure out how Riley had built what Riley had built.

The trick was not the phrase.

The trick was that Riley believed the phrase.

The trick was that the phrase made everyone in the locker room believe the phrase.

The trick was that the phrase, by April, became a real piece of equipment that could be brought to the Boston Garden in June and used, in the way that a chisel is used, to break the parquet.

What Riley figured out, over the next nine years, was the modern coaching profession.

The coach as CEO.

The coach as brand.

The coach as the public face of the franchise’s competitive identity.

The coach as, in the language of the technical age that came after him, the system architect of the team’s whole culture.

He invented it on the fly.

The league has been copying it ever since.

It was the next thing.

It was Showtime.

Chapter 6

The Boston Problem

June 12, 1984. Boston Garden. Game Seven of the NBA Finals, fourth quarter, three minutes and thirty seconds remaining, the Boston Celtics leading the Los Angeles Lakers by six, the temperature on the parquet — a parquet that the Boston Garden management has, for reasons later to be debated, declined to air-condition for any of the Boston-played Finals games of the previous two weeks — at approximately ninety-six degrees Fahrenheit, and Earvin Magic Johnson Jr., age twenty-four, the Lakers’ point guard and the heart of the franchise’s offensive identity, dribbling the ball off his own foot and out of bounds in front of his own bench.

The turnover was the sixth of his game.

The crowd at the Garden — fifteen thousand mostly local, mostly white, mostly middle-aged Boston basketball fans who have been waiting fifteen years for an excuse to humiliate the team from California — produces a noise that, on the local CBS broadcast tape later, sounds something like a prison-yard cheer the moment after a fight has been won.

The Lakers, three minutes and thirty seconds later, will lose the game.

The Lakers, on the morning of June 13, 1984, will fly home to Los Angeles having just lost the NBA Finals, in seven games, to the Boston Celtics, for the eighth time in the eight Finals series the two franchises have played against each other since the 1950s — a record of zero wins and eight losses, an unbroken eight-Finals losing streak, dating back to the Bob Cousy and Bill Russell Celtics teams of the late nineteen-fifties.

The Lakers, in 1984, are zero-and-eight against Boston in the Finals.

That is the Boston Problem.

The Boston Problem is what this chapter is about.

The Boston Problem is what the entire 1984–85 Lakers season will be about.

The Boston Problem is what, by the spring of 1985, will be solved — and what, in being solved, will produce the breakthrough that turns Showtime from a regular-season novelty act into the championship-machine the era is now remembered as.

But on the morning of June 13, 1984, with Magic on the team plane back to LAX, with a Boston Globe on his lap whose back-page headline reads, in eighteen-point bold black type, TRAGIC, the Boston Problem looks unsolvable.

It does not look unsolvable for one simple reason.

It looks unsolvable for at least four.

The first reason is Larry Bird.

Larry Bird, in June of 1984, has just won the NBA Finals MVP award. He has averaged twenty-seven points and fourteen rebounds against the Lakers across the seven games of the Finals. He has shot fifty-four percent from the field. He has — in Game Five at the Garden, the famous Heat Game, when the temperature in the building reached an alleged ninety-seven degrees and Kareem played the second half breathing oxygen from a tank between possessions — outplayed every Laker in uniform across the full forty-eight minutes. He has, in the language of professional basketball, taken Magic Johnson’s series and put it in his pocket.

Bird is twenty-seven.

Bird’s summer following the 1984 Finals is described in his subsequent published autobiography and biographical accounts as a sustained off-season basketball workout in his hometown of French Lick, Indiana — where he had remained living, in the off-seasons, throughout his pro career.

That is the first reason.

The first reason is that Larry Bird, in June of 1984, is the best basketball player on the planet.

The second reason is the Boston frontcourt.

The Boston frontcourt, in June of 1984, is Bird at the three, Kevin McHale at the four, and Robert Parish at the five. McHale is a six-foot-eleven Minnesotan with a low-post arsenal that includes — by McHale’s own count, which he has shared with the press, in the way he shares everything with the press — eight different fadeaway moves, which he calls, individually, by name, like a chef itemizing the dishes on a tasting menu. Parish is the seven-foot center, the steadiest free-throw shooter on the team, the long-armed shot-blocker behind the McHale-and-Bird wall. The three of them, together, are the best frontcourt in the league. The three of them, together, will play in three more Finals against the Lakers across the next four seasons.

The third reason is Red Auerbach.

Red Auerbach, in June of 1984, is sixty-six years old, the president of the Boston Celtics, the architect of fourteen NBA championships, and the man who has been smoking a victory cigar on the bench at the end of every Boston championship clincher since the Eisenhower administration. He runs the franchise. He has run the franchise since 1950. He has hired, fired, drafted, traded, and emotionally sustained every Boston champion in the post-war era. He is the most successful executive in the history of American professional team sports. He is, in 1984, in the absolute peak of his powers.

The fourth reason is the Garden itself.

The Boston Garden, in 1984, is a fifty-six-year-old bandbox at the corner of Causeway and Canal in downtown Boston, with a parquet floor laid in 1946 from cuts of red oak that have, in the intervening thirty-eight years, developed a series of dead spots — places where a basketball, dribbled, will not return cleanly to the dribbler’s hand. The dead spots are a closely guarded part of the Boston playbook. Bird knows where they are. McHale knows where they are. Parish knows where they are. Visiting players, including the Lakers, do not. Visiting players who attempt to dribble through certain regions of the Garden floor in the third quarter of a Finals game, with the crowd at full volume, find their dribble suddenly and inexplicably stopped, the basketball going dead at their feet, the home team’s defenders descending on the suddenly stationary visitor for the steal.

That is the fourth reason.

The Garden cheats.

The Lakers, on the morning of June 13, 1984, are not just zero-and-eight against the Celtics in the Finals. They are zero-and-eight against the Celtics in the Finals in a league in which the Celtics have, in their own home arena, a literal piece of cheating equipment built into the floor of the building.

This is, the Lakers tell themselves on the plane home, the structural reason.

This is, the Lakers tell themselves, why they have lost.

But the Lakers, on the plane home, also know that this is not the structural reason.

The structural reason is Magic.

The structural reason is that Magic Johnson, in the 1984 Finals, has played the worst basketball of his professional career to that point. He has made critical mistakes in late-game situations that have, in two of the seven games, given the Celtics possessions they converted into wins. The Boston papers, in the days after Game Seven, tagged him Tragic Johnson.

Tragic Johnson, the Globe ran.

Tragic, the Globe headline ran, in the simpler, harsher form, on the morning of June 13.

Magic, twenty-four years old, the most charismatic athlete in his sport, the public face of the franchise, the architect of Showtime, has just been called Tragic on the back page of the largest newspaper in New England.

Magic, twenty-four years old, sits on the team plane back to LAX with the back page of the Boston Globe in his lap, and he reads it.

He reads it again.

He reads it a third time.

He folds the paper. He puts the paper in his briefcase. He looks out the window. The plane is somewhere over Wyoming. The plane has six hours to go.

What he is thinking, in those six hours, is not on the public record. What he did with the next eleven months is.

Magic Johnson, between June 13, 1984 and June 9, 1985, a period of three hundred and sixty-two days, produced the single most concentrated personal basketball reinvention in the modern history of the sport.

He arrived at Lakers training camp in October of 1984 having reportedly worked out, that summer, between five and six hours a day, six days a week, at UCLA’s Pauley Pavilion, with a personal trainer Pat Riley had quietly arranged.

By all accounts published since, Magic’s, Riley’s, and the Lakers’ beat reporters’, Magic returned to camp leaner, faster, and more focused than the season before, having spent his summer in a sustained reconstruction of his own conditioning.

The Lakers’ coaching staff, across the same off-season, came back with two adjustments. The first was tactical: the Celtics had figured out how to defend the Lakers’ transition game, by clogging the lanes that Magic relied on to throw the no-look pass. The 1984–85 Lakers would need a more complete half-court offense to run when the transition game was bottled up.

The second was structural: the 1984 Lakers had been, by Riley’s published assessment, too soft against the Celtics’ physicality. The 1984–85 Lakers were going to play with more contact.

That was the plan.

The plan would be tested in eleven months.

The plan would, against most expectations, work.

But that is the next chapter.

What was true, on June 13, 1984, on the team plane to LAX, with the Boston Globe folded in Magic’s briefcase, was that the franchise’s eight Finals losses to the Celtics in eight tries had become a load-bearing structural feature of the franchise’s identity.

Until the franchise broke that streak, every championship the Lakers won would be marked, in the historical record and in the basketball-historical conversation, with an implicit asterisk — yes, but they have never beaten Boston for the title.

Until the franchise broke that streak, Magic Johnson’s career would be marked with the same asterisk — yes, but he could not beat Bird in the Garden.

Until the franchise broke that streak, Buss’s whole project — the Forum Club, the Laker Girls, the Showtime branding, the celebrity courtside, the entire modern apparatus he had been constructing since the spring of 1979, would be marked with the same asterisk, yes, but they could not, on a parquet floor in Boston, in front of a crowd that hated them, beat the team that had been doing it for thirty years.

The 1984–85 Lakers, on the plane home, knew this.

The 1984–85 Lakers knew exactly what they had to do.

The 1984–85 Lakers were going to do it.

The Boston Problem was, by the spring of 1985, going to be solved.

The Boston Problem was, in the spring of 1985, going to be solved in Boston.

The Boston Problem was, in the spring of 1985, going to be solved by an aging center named Kareem Abdul-Jabbar, who would turn thirty-eight years old in the middle of the Finals, in front of a crowd of fifteen thousand Boston fans who had been waiting fifteen years for an excuse to humiliate the team from California.

But that, also, is the next chapter.

For now, on the morning of June 13, 1984, in the dark cabin of a westbound 727, the Lakers are eight-and-zero in Finals losses to Boston, and Magic is reading a headline that calls him Tragic, and the entire flight home is quiet.

The flight home will be quiet, in fact, for forty-eight hours after landing.

By the third day, training camp for the next season has, in everyone’s heads, already begun.

By the third day, the next chapter has already started.

By the third day, eleven months from June 9, 1985, the Lakers are getting ready.

Chapter 7

The Garden

May 27, 1985. Boston Garden. Memorial Day. Game One of the NBA Finals. Final score: Boston Celtics one hundred and forty-eight, Los Angeles Lakers one hundred and fourteen. The thirty-four-point margin is, at the time of the game, the largest margin of defeat in any NBA Finals game ever played.

The Lakers, on the morning of May 28, 1985, are zero and one in the Finals.

The morning headlines in Boston call it the Memorial Day Massacre.

The morning headlines in Los Angeles call it nothing, because the morning newspapers in Los Angeles have not yet figured out what to call it. The Lakers’ veterans, in the locker room after the game, are subdued. Magic, in the locker room after the game, refuses to come to the press. Kareem, in the locker room after the game, sits on a stool in the corner with his head between his knees, in a way that the team’s beat reporters have not, in five seasons of covering him, ever previously seen.

Kareem is thirty-eight years old.

Kareem has been the Lakers’ starting center for ten years.

Kareem has, in the previous twelve hours, been outscored, outrebounded, outhustled, and outplayed at every single position on the floor by Robert Parish, who is two years younger than him, six inches taller in his prime, and getting better.

In the days between Game One and Game Two, by Riley’s and Kareem’s separately published recollections, the team’s plan changed: the offense was going to run through Kareem from the low post, every possession the Lakers could engineer. He was thirty-eight years old and the Lakers had four games to win.

The Lakers, in the next four games, do exactly what the conversation specified.

They go to Boston, in Game Six, on June 9, 1985, and they win one hundred and eleven to one hundred. Kareem scores twenty-nine points and pulls down eleven rebounds. Magic, with the Boston Garden crowd at full volume, posts an immaculate triple-double — fourteen points, fourteen assists, ten rebounds. The Lakers shoot fifty-eight percent from the floor. The Celtics, despite Bird’s twenty-eight points, shoot forty-three.

It is the first time in the eight-decade history of the Boston Celtics franchise that another team has clinched a championship on the parquet floor of the Boston Garden.

It is the first time in the franchise-on-franchise history of Lakers-Celtics Finals, eight previous series, all Boston wins, dating back to 1959, that the team from Los Angeles has come out on the right end of a series.

It is, in the language of the franchise, the breakthrough.

It is, in the language of the city of Los Angeles, the night the Lakers stopped being the team that couldn’t beat Boston and became the team that did.

It is, on a forty-eight-degrees-Fahrenheit Sunday evening in late spring in New England, the moment that the Boston Problem — the structural feature of Lakers history that had calcified the franchise’s championship identity from 1959 through 1984 — was, in the technical sense, dissolved.

It is, in the long-running internal vocabulary the franchise had been carrying since 1959, the night the Boston Problem was solved.

To understand why Game Six in Boston was the game that Lakers fans of the era still talk about — even now, even in 2026, even in conversations between fathers and sons in Pasadena and Whittier and Glendora and El Monte that begin with the phrase no, the one I want to tell you about is ‘85 — you have to back up to the conversation on the team bus, and you have to listen to it again, and you have to understand what Kareem was actually saying.

Kareem was saying: I am still here.

Kareem was saying: I am thirty-eight, and I am the best center in this series, and you have not used me.

Kareem was saying: use me.

Riley used him.

Game Two, two days after the Memorial Day Massacre, Kareem received the ball in the low post on every Lakers possession that the team could engineer. He scored thirty points. He had seventeen rebounds. He blocked eight shots. The Lakers won, one hundred and nine to one hundred and two. The Series, on the morning of June first, was tied at one game apiece. The Lakers, on the team plane home, felt, for the first time in eleven months, like champions.

Game Three, in Los Angeles, the Lakers won by twenty-five.

Game Four, in Los Angeles, the Celtics won — barely, by three, in overtime, with Bird scoring nineteen of his thirty in the fourth quarter.

Game Five, in Los Angeles, the Lakers won by eight.

Game Six, in Boston, the Lakers won by eleven.

The Series was over.

The Boston Problem was over.

The Lakers were champions.

Kareem was Finals MVP, at thirty-eight years old, becoming the oldest player ever to win the award.

Kareem was, on the night of June 9, 1985, in the visitors’ locker room at the Boston Garden, smiling — for the first time, several Lakers later said, that any of them had seen since the 1980 championship.

He was holding a glass of champagne.

The Boston Problem was solved.

What is harder to explain, sixty-some years later, is what Game Six in the Garden meant to the city of Los Angeles, on the evening of June 9, 1985, in the way the city remembered it on the morning of June 10, 1985, and the morning of June 11, 1985, and every morning thereafter for the next thirty-five years.

It meant the city had won.

The city had not, in any prior moment of its athletic history, won like that.

The city had won the World Series in 1959, in 1963, in 1965, and in 1981 — but baseball is, in its essential structure, a regional sport that does not consolidate civic identity in the way a basketball championship does. The city had won the Super Bowl, in the Rams’ single Super Bowl appearance, never. The city had won the Stanley Cup, with the Kings, never. The city had, in basketball, won championships in 1972 (Wilt and West, against the Knicks) and 1980 (Magic at center, in Philadelphia). But it had not, in any of those championships, won against Boston.

Boston was the rival.

Boston was the standard.

Boston was, in the language the city of Los Angeles had been using about itself since the late 1940s, the older, colder, more serious, more historical city that Los Angeles was, in the popular imagination of both cities and of the country in general, supposed to feel inferior to.

Boston had, in the winter of 1985, openly mocked Los Angeles.

The Boston Globe, in its annual spring preview of the upcoming Finals, had run a section called Why the Lakers Cannot Win in Boston, which had run for two pages, and which had cited, as supporting evidence, the previous eight series, the parquet, the cigars, the ghost of Russell, the ghost of Cousy, the ghost of Heinsohn, the ghost of every Boston Celtic from 1957 forward, and which had concluded with a paragraph beginning the Lakers’ problem is structural.

The Lakers’ problem was, on the night of June 9, 1985, dissolved.

The Lakers’ problem had, on the night of June 9, 1985, been resolved by an aging center who had been told to ask for the ball, and who had asked, and who had received, and who had — in his thirty-eighth-birthday season, in his sixteenth professional season, in his eleventh season as a Laker — out-played one of the great centers of the era for six straight games on the floor of the building that had been built to humiliate him.

The team came home that night to a city that had, for thirty-five years, been the loser in this matchup. The grievance — that Boston was the franchise that could win against the franchise from the West Coast — was, on the night of June 9, 1985, settled.

The city had its first Boston ring.

The Showtime era, in the technical sense, started here.

The 1980 championship had been the first ring under the new ownership — the rookie season, the Magic-plays-center game, the franchise establishing its modern identity. But the 1985 championship was the ring that defined the new identity, in the comparative sense — the ring that proved, against the team that had previously decided what a franchise could and could not be, that the new franchise could be, and was, the better franchise.

That ring lived, until Buss’s death in 2013, in a glass case in his office on the fifth floor of the Forum.

That ring confirmed what every fan in southern California had suspected since 1980, which was that the team from their city had become, against the long Boston shadow, the best team in the league.

It would stay there for the next several years.

Chapter 8

The Guarantee

There is a sentence professional coaches in major American team sports do not utter.

The sentence is a public, on-the-record promise, made in the immediate aftermath of winning a championship, that the same team will win the same championship again in the next season. The sentence has not been uttered, in any of the four major American professional leagues, more than half a dozen times in the last eighty years, and on all of the occasions on which it has been uttered, it has, without exception, in the language of professional sports superstition, brought the curse. The team that has uttered it has, in every prior case, failed to repeat the championship the following season. The coach who has uttered it has, in every prior case, regretted the utterance.

This is, in the locker-room culture of professional team sports, the unwritten law.

You do not say it.

You do not say it on national television.

You do not say it during a championship parade.

You do not say it within range of a single live microphone, on the floor of the Forum, in the moments after the buzzer of the Game Six clinching victory, while the cameras are still on, with five hundred reporters in the room, with Magic Johnson standing two feet to your left.

Pat Riley said it.

The day was June 14, 1987. The venue was the Forum. The Lakers had just defeated the Boston Celtics, four games to two, to win their fourth NBA championship of the 1980s. Magic Johnson had just been named Finals MVP for the second time in his career. The Forum’s championship trophy ceremony was in progress. Riley had just been handed the microphone by NBA commissioner David Stern. The crowd was at full volume. The city was watching, on national television, on a Sunday evening in primetime, with most of the country tuned in for the first time all postseason because CBS had finally given the NBA Finals the Sunday-night-prime-time slot that the league had been requesting for a decade.

Riley said it.

He said: I guarantee that the Los Angeles Lakers will repeat as champions next season.

He said it once.

He did not soften it.

He did not say I think or I believe or we’ll try to or we have a chance to. He said I guarantee, in the declarative, with the indicative, in front of fifteen thousand people in the building, with cameras on, in front of five hundred reporters in the press scrum, with the Boston Celtics’ Larry Bird, Kevin McHale, and Robert Parish watching on a flight back to Logan, with the entire Eastern Conference of the National Basketball Association suddenly aware that the head coach of the team that had just beaten them had publicly committed his roster to a back-to-back.

Magic, in published interviews across the decades since, has told the story of standing two feet to Riley’s left when the Guarantee was issued and immediately understanding that the burden of delivering on it had been placed on the team for the next eleven months. That, in Magic’s account, was exactly Riley’s plan.

Riley’s plan, as he subsequently explained it in his 1993 book The Winner Within and in interviews across the next thirty years, was to use the public guarantee as a mechanism for doing what professional team sports usually find impossible to do, which is win two championships in a row.

The mechanism, Riley argued, was psychological.

The defending champion, in any given league, had, across decades of evidence, a documented tendency to win the regular season after a championship at a slightly worse rate than they had won the regular season before the championship. The reason, Riley said, was complacency. Complacency arose because the previous championship had already been won, and the players’ incentive structure, the contract, the celebrity, the residual glow, was largely satisfied. Complacency was, as Riley put it in one of the better passages of The Winner Within, the rust on the engine. Complacency, left untreated, would corrode a championship roster from the inside, possession by possession, late-season game by late-season game, until, when the playoffs came around again, the roster would not have the gear left to repeat.

The treatment for complacency, in Riley’s framework, was a public, externally imposed expectation that the players could not, even psychologically, dismiss.

The Guarantee, in Riley’s framework, was the treatment.

He had not invented this idea. He had stolen it, by his own admission, from the chess master Bobby Fischer, who had once, before a championship match, written on a blackboard in his hotel room I will win. He had stolen it from the boxer Muhammad Ali, who had announced his championship victories before they happened. He had stolen it from his old college coach, Adolph Rupp, who had publicly committed Kentucky teams to undefeated seasons in October, and who had, sometimes, delivered. The Guarantee was a tool. The tool was a public commitment, made in advance, for the purpose of forcing the speaker into the work that would be required to make the commitment true.

Riley used the tool on June 14, 1987.

The tool worked.

The 1987–88 Lakers, with the Guarantee published in every newspaper in the country and every locker room in the league as their summer wallpaper, played the regular season as a team that knew it was being judged against a single line. They won sixty-two games. They finished first in the Western Conference. They went into the playoffs as the defending champions and the public favorites and the team that had, by the head coach’s own admission, on the floor of the building, in primetime, committed to a back-to-back.

The playoffs went seven games in three of four rounds.

The first round, against the San Antonio Spurs, the Lakers swept three to nothing.

The second round, against the Utah Jazz of John Stockton and Karl Malone, went seven games. The Lakers won.

The Western Conference Finals, against the Dallas Mavericks of Mark Aguirre and Rolando Blackman, went seven games. The Lakers won.

The NBA Finals, against the Detroit Pistons of Isiah Thomas and Joe Dumars and Bill Laimbeer, went, as we will see in the next chapter, seven games. The Lakers won.

Riley did not, in the eight remaining years of his career as the Lakers’ head coach (and the Knicks’ and the Heat’s after that), publicly guarantee a championship again.

The Guarantee, like a mortgage, had been issued once, paid once, and retired.

But the Guarantee, in the eleven months between June 14, 1987 and June 21, 1988, had done its work.

It had, by Riley’s own count, in The Winner Within, turned a roster that should have been collapsing under the weight of its previous championship into a roster that was, instead, propelled by a single public sentence into the most physically demanding, most emotionally exhausting, most psychologically loaded postseason of the modern NBA era, and that had — at the end of seven games against the Detroit Pistons, in the Forum, on a Sunday evening in late June, won.

That is the chapter about the Guarantee.

The next chapter is the chapter about what the Guarantee had to deliver.

Magic Johnson, who had stood two feet to Riley’s left when the Guarantee was issued, told the story for the rest of his career.

He told it in commencement addresses.

He told it in motivational speeches.

He told it in his autobiography.

The story, in his telling, was about leadership.

The story, in Riley’s telling, was about psychology.

The story, in the basketball-historical record’s telling, is about the moment a head coach in a major American professional league did the thing his profession’s culture said you do not do, and was right to do it, and got away with it, and won.

The story is also, incidentally, about the way Showtime, by the spring of 1987, had become the team that did things other teams did not do. Other teams did not have nightclubs in the arena. Other teams did not have celebrity courtside seating as a marketing channel. Other teams did not have head coaches who issued public championship guarantees on national television in primetime.

Showtime did all of those things.

Showtime, by the spring of 1987, was a different kind of franchise from the other twenty-two franchises in the league.

The Guarantee was a piece of that.

The Guarantee was, in the long view, the moment Showtime stopped being a basketball team and started being something the league had not previously had, which was a culture.

The culture was the basketball.

The culture was also the night.

The culture was also the head coach who would, in the right moment, on the right microphone, stand on the right floor and say the sentence the rest of the league would not say.

That, in the language of the era, was Showtime.

The Guarantee was Showtime.

Chapter 9

Back-to-Back

The Detroit Pistons, in June of 1988, were not the team you wanted in the Finals if you had a championship guarantee to deliver.

The Detroit Pistons were called the Bad Boys.

The Detroit Pistons had, at the small forward position, a six-foot-eight Croatian-American power forward named Rick Mahorn, who would have been a center on any other team in the league but who, on the Pistons, was the third-largest body in the starting lineup. The Detroit Pistons had, at the center position, a six-foot-eleven Notre Dame graduate named Bill Laimbeer, who threw elbows on every possession and who, when caught throwing the elbow on the broadcast tape, fell to the floor as though he had been the one who had been hit. The Detroit Pistons had, on the bench, a six-foot-seven undrafted forward from Oklahoma named Dennis Rodman, who would, in the Finals’ run-up, become so famous for his physical play that the league office sent the Pistons a memo, mid-season, asking the team to consider the league’s image. The Pistons had, at the point guard position, a six-foot-one Indianapolis-born ball player named Isiah Thomas, who had grown up in the same Chicago neighborhood as Magic Johnson and had been, in a Lakers-versus-Pistons regular-season tradition, exchanging a ceremonial pre-game kiss on the cheek with Magic before tip-off for the past four seasons.

The Detroit Pistons were coached by Chuck Daly, who wore a gray suit, smoked between quarters, and had, by the testimony of every player on his roster, the highest tolerance for hard contact of any head coach in the league.

The Detroit Pistons, in June of 1988, were going to make Riley’s Guarantee very difficult to deliver.

The series went seven games.

Game One, at the Forum, the Pistons won, 105–93. The Pistons’ first NBA Finals victory in the franchise’s thirty-one-year history. The Forum, on the morning of June 8, 1988, was very quiet.

Game Two, at the Forum, the Lakers won, 108–96. The series tied at one. Magic, in the postgame, told the press: we are not going to lose this series.

Game Three, in Detroit, the Pistons won, 99–86. The Pistons up two games to one.

Game Four, in Detroit, the Lakers won, 86–82, in a low-scoring, foul-heavy, elbow-heavy game that more closely resembled, by neutral observers’ descriptions, a hockey contest than a basketball game. The series tied at two.

Game Five, in Detroit, the Pistons won, 104–94. The Pistons up three games to two.

The Lakers, after Game Five, had to win two consecutive games, Game Six in Detroit and Game Seven in Los Angeles, to retain the championship.

Or the Guarantee was going to fail.

Or Riley’s whole psychological architecture for the season, the idea that public expectations could be turned into private effort, was going to come apart in Detroit on a Sunday afternoon in mid-June.

Game Six was on a Sunday.

Game Six was at the Pontiac Silverdome, the indoor stadium where the Pistons played their home games in the 1980s, with a paid attendance of forty-one thousand seven hundred and thirty-two — the largest crowd ever to attend an NBA Finals game up to that point.

Game Six, on the afternoon of June 19, 1988, was the game the Detroit Pistons should have won.

The Detroit Pistons led by three points with one minute and seventeen seconds left in regulation.

Isiah Thomas, on a sprained right ankle that had seized in the third quarter and that, by the fourth quarter, had visibly swelled to twice its normal size, had, across the third quarter alone, scored twenty-five points on the Lakers’ defense, including eleven consecutive points in the middle of the quarter that had given the Pistons a fourteen-point lead heading into the fourth.

The lead had collapsed.

It had collapsed for the simplest reason a lead in a basketball game collapses, which is that the team that is about to lose stops making shots.

The Pistons, in the fourth quarter of Game Six, missed nine consecutive field-goal attempts from the floor. The Lakers, in the same minutes, made eight of their last twelve.

With one minute and seventeen seconds left, with the Pistons leading by three, with Isiah Thomas standing at the free-throw line with the chance to put his team up by five, with the Forum-pumped Pontiac crowd on its feet and screaming, the Lakers took a timeout.

In the timeout, Riley drew a play that ran Kareem to the low post, Worthy to the corner, and Magic to the top of the key with the ball.

The play, when it came out of the timeout, did not run as drawn.

The play, when it came out of the timeout, ran as a Magic Johnson drive to the basket that drew a foul from Bill Laimbeer with fourteen seconds left in regulation.

Magic Johnson, with fourteen seconds left, in the loudest basketball arena in the United States that afternoon, on a Sunday in June, with the Detroit Pistons leading by three points and a public guarantee on the line, stepped to the free-throw line and made both shots.

Lakers down one.

Detroit, on the inbound, ran twelve seconds off the clock and turned the ball over.

The Lakers, with two seconds left, ran a play that ended in a Kareem skyhook from the right elbow that Bill Laimbeer fouled — incidentally or deliberately, depending on which retelling, although the official scorers’ record says deliberately, at the moment of the release. The shot did not count. The foul did. Kareem, age forty-one, with the championship guarantee on the line, with the team trailing by one and one second left on the clock and the Pontiac Silverdome at full volume, walked to the free-throw line.

He shot the first.

Make.

He shot the second.

Make.

Lakers up one.

The Pistons inbounded with one second left.

Joe Dumars launched a thirty-foot jumper.

Miss.

The Lakers had won Game Six.

The series was tied at three games apiece.

Game Seven was at the Forum.

Game Seven was on Tuesday, June 21, 1988.

Game Seven, at the Forum, in front of a crowd that had been waiting for a back-to-back championship for the past eleven months, was won by the Lakers, 108–105.

The Pistons led for most of the second half. The Lakers led for the last seven minutes. Magic finished the game with nineteen points, fourteen assists, and three rebounds. Worthy finished the game with thirty-six points, sixteen rebounds, and ten assists — a triple-double, the only triple-double of his career, on the right night, in front of the right crowd, in the right game.

Worthy was Finals MVP.

Kareem played thirty-one minutes at age forty-one and scored fourteen points.

The Lakers had repeated.

The first NBA franchise to repeat as champions since the 1968 and 1969 Boston Celtics, a span of nineteen years, was the Lakers.

The Boston Problem had been solved in 1985.

The repeat-champion problem had been solved in 1988.

The franchise had now done both.

Showtime, in June of 1988, was at its absolute height.

What is harder to write about, sixty-some years after the fact, is what the Detroit Pistons looked like coming off that floor.

The Detroit Pistons did not, on the night of June 21, 1988, walk off the floor disappointed. The Detroit Pistons walked off the floor angry. The Detroit Pistons, by their published accounts in interviews and books across the next thirty years, had decided, somewhere in the locker room after Game Seven, that the next time they were in the Finals, which they correctly assumed would be the 1989 Finals, against the same Lakers, they were going to win.

The Detroit Pistons, by those same accounts, were going to win in 1989 by being more physical than they had been in 1988.

The Detroit Pistons did, in fact, win in 1989. They won in a four-game sweep, against a Lakers team that had begun the Finals with both Magic Johnson and Byron Scott injured.

The 1989 Lakers had no chance.

The 1989 Lakers were swept.

But the 1989 sweep is not in this book, and the 1989 sweep is not a chapter, because the 1989 sweep is not part of the Showtime era as that era is generally understood. The Showtime era, as that era is generally understood, ends in June of 1988, with the back-to-back championships, with Worthy’s triple-double, with Kareem’s two free throws in Game Six in Pontiac, with Magic’s fourteen assists in Game Seven, and with the Forum’s ovation as the team gathered on the floor for the trophy.

The Showtime era ends, in the long view, on the night the Guarantee was paid.

Eleven months.

Five championships in ten years.

The most successful single decade any NBA franchise has ever produced.

Chapter 10

Showtime as Style

What Buss had built, by the spring of 1988, was no longer a basketball franchise.

What Buss had built was an aesthetic.

The aesthetic — by then established, refined, and exported to the rest of the National Basketball Association in piecemeal copies that other franchises would spend the better part of the next thirty years assembling into their own knockoff versions of the original — was the entire visible surface of what an NBA game could be made to look like, on a Tuesday evening in primetime, on a national television broadcast, in front of a paying crowd, in a building owned by a man who understood that the basketball was the centerpiece but not the whole table.

The aesthetic had four working parts.

The first part was the lighting.

The Forum’s lighting, before Buss bought the building, had been the standard NBA arena lighting of the era — a uniform house-light wash, evenly distributed across the playing surface, designed to give every fan in every section the same general visual access to the game. Under Buss the lighting was reworked across the early seasons of his ownership: concentrated on the floor itself, dimmed in the seating bowl, isolating the players in a kind of theatrical spotlight, producing, for the television cameras, but also for the in-arena experience, the visual signature that every American sports broadcast for the next forty-five years would attempt to reproduce.

The signature was: bright floor, dark seats. The signature was: the players in the spotlight, the crowd in shadow.

The other twenty-one franchises in the league, in the years that followed, copied the lighting one by one. Boston resisted longest — the Boston Garden retained its uniform house-light wash through the late 1980s, in the same way the Boston Garden retained its parquet — but by the early 1990s, every NBA arena, including the Garden’s eventual replacement at the new TD Banknorth Garden, had adopted the bright-floor, dark-seats convention.

The convention had originated in Inglewood, in the early years of Buss’s ownership.

The second part was the music.

The Forum’s pre-game and timeout music, before Buss bought the building, had been an organ, in the standard NBA-arena fashion of the era. Under Buss, recorded popular music began to share the soundscape, played in the breaks alongside or instead of the organ.

The disc jockey’s job was to play recorded popular music.

The recorded popular music, across the 1979–80 season, was a tentative thing. The Forum’s sound system had not been designed for it. The crowd, conditioned to the organ, did not, at first, respond. By the spring of 1981, after Magic’s first championship and after the Forum’s renovation of its sound system, the in-arena music had become a fixture. By the spring of 1982, with the lighting and the music both on the new system, the Forum had become, by the description of every visiting writer who wrote about it, not so much a basketball arena as a club.

The club analogy was correct.

The club analogy was, as an analogy, the actual marketing direction Buss had given to the Forum’s operations staff in 1979 in their first organizational meeting.

He had said: make this place feel like the Whisky.

The Whisky-A-Go-Go, on Sunset, was the most famous rock club in Los Angeles in 1979, and had been since the mid-1960s, when the Doors had played as the house band.

The Forum, by the spring of 1982, felt like the Whisky.

Other arenas, in the years that followed, copied the music one by one.

By the early 1990s, every NBA arena in the league played recorded popular music in place of the organ.

The organ-only arena was extinct.

The Forum had killed it.

The third part was the Laker Girls.

The Laker Girls — professional dancers in matching gold-and-purple uniforms, choreographed in routines designed for the breaks of an NBA game — were Buss’s invention, assembled in the autumn of 1979 and on the floor for the team’s home opener of the 1979–80 season. The squad was the first dance team in the NBA.

The Laker Girls would, across the 1980s, develop into a brand within a brand. Their choreographer for several years in the early-to-mid 1980s was a young Paula Abdul, who would later become a popular musician in her own right.

By the end of the 1980s, every team in the NBA had a dance squad.

By the end of the 1990s, the dance squad had become, in every major American professional team-sports league, a standard fixture of the in-game experience.

That, too, was Buss’s.

That, too, had originated in 1979.

The fourth part was the celebrity.

The celebrity — the photograph of Jack Nicholson in sunglasses in courtside seat number 14 in section A1, the photograph of Dyan Cannon in courtside seat number 12, the photograph of John McEnroe attending playoff games during his own off-court hours, the photograph of Frank Sinatra and Don Rickles and Walter Matthau and Lou Adler and Penny Marshall and James Brolin and Magic’s mother Christine Johnson and Kareem’s father Ferdinand Lewis Alcindor and the rotating constellation of recording-industry executives and talent agents and television actors and tennis players and professional women who occupied the front rows of the Forum’s seating bowl on every game night across the decade — was the photograph that, in the marketing-department sense, sold the Forum to the rest of the country.

The photograph also sold the franchise.

The photograph also sold the city.

The photograph was, in the long view, the marketing apparatus that turned the Lakers into a national brand at a moment when the rest of the league was still a regional one.

In 1979, the NBA was a regional sport.

By 1990, the NBA was a global one.

The Lakers had been a great deal of the reason.

The Lakers had been a great deal of the reason because Buss had, since 1979, been carefully constructing the conditions under which the photograph would be taken — the lighting, the music, the dance squad, the celebrity courtside, the Forum Club after the games — and the photograph, once taken, would do the marketing for him.

The photograph, in the long view, was the apparatus.

The apparatus was, in the long view, Showtime.

The Lakers won championships across the decade because Magic and Kareem were the best two players in the league at their positions, and because Riley was the best coach in the league at the way the league was being played, and because Buss had assembled around them the supporting roster that allowed the two stars to play their best basketball, and because the franchise’s organizational culture, set by Buss, enforced by Riley, embodied by Magic and Kareem, was, across the entire decade, the most professional culture in the NBA.

The basketball, in this view, won the championships.

But the basketball would not, on its own, have made the franchise into the cultural fact it became.

The cultural fact required the lighting. The cultural fact required the music.

The cultural fact required, over time, that Buss build, in Inglewood, a building that did not, on game nights, feel like an arena, but rather like the inside of a televised version of the city of Los Angeles itself — bright, fast, sun-drenched, brand-aware, gold-and-purple, well-soundtracked, and, above all else, seen.

That is what Showtime was.

That is what the rest of the league copied.

That is what every NBA arena in 2026 still looks like.

The basketball, in 2026, has changed.

The basketball, in 2026, is faster, more three-point-oriented, more analytics-driven, more positionless than the basketball Magic and Kareem played.

But the lighting, the music, the dance squad, the celebrity courtside, the in-arena production, the Showtime aesthetic, in the strict architectural sense, has not changed.

The Showtime aesthetic is what every NBA franchise sells, every night, to every paying customer in every building in every city in the league.

The Showtime aesthetic was a gift Buss left to the league when he died in 2013.

The league has not, in the years since, given the gift back.

The league still uses it.

The league will, in all likelihood, use it forever.

That, in the end, is what Buss built.

That, over time, is the chapter.

Chapter 11

The Cocaine Years

This is the chapter that is hardest to write.

It is hardest to write because it touches the part of the era that the era’s surviving principals, Magic, Kareem, Riley, the Buss family, have spent the better part of forty years trying to keep at the periphery of the public record. It is hardest to write because the documentary base for it is uneven: parts of the story are exhaustively documented in the autobiographies of the players who admitted to it, parts of the story are inferential at best, and parts of the story are the kind of rumor that, under the Prime Directive that governs this book, must be cut entirely.

What is going to be in this chapter, accordingly, is what is documented.

What is documented is the Spencer Haywood story.

What is documented is the league-wide cocaine problem of the early 1980s.

What is documented is the specific intersection of the two with the Lakers’ 1979–80 championship run.

What is not going to be in this chapter is anything I cannot anchor to a primary source — a published autobiography, a contemporary newspaper account, a DEA filing, a court record, a transcript of an on-the-record interview. Where the historical record is silent, the chapter will be silent. Where the historical record speculates, the chapter will not speculate. Where a name is not in the documented record, the name will not be in this chapter.

The chapter is, in this sense, narrower than the story.

The chapter is, in this sense, the documented version of the story.

That is the only version that can be written.

Spencer Haywood was the Lakers’ starting power forward at the start of the 1979–80 season.

He had come to the Lakers in October of 1979 in a trade from the New Orleans Jazz. He was thirty years old. He had been the second overall pick in the 1971 NBA draft, taken by the Buffalo Braves, although his earlier ABA contract and the resulting court case, Haywood v. National Basketball Association, 401 U.S. 1204 (1971), had cleared the legal pathway for college players to enter the NBA before completing a four-year degree, and would, in the long view, become the foundational legal precedent for every player the league had drafted since. Haywood’s case was, in the strict legal sense, the case that made it possible for Magic Johnson, eight years later, to leave Michigan State after his sophomore year and enter the NBA draft.

Haywood, by 1979, was a four-time NBA All-Star, an Olympic gold medalist (1968, Mexico City), and a player who had played, for various teams, for nine professional seasons before the trade to the Lakers.

He was also, in his 1992 autobiography The Rise, the Fall, the Recovery, in the depths of a cocaine addiction that he had developed across the previous two seasons in New York, with the Knicks, and that had, by the autumn of 1979, reached the kind of clinical severity that a major-league team’s training staff could no longer fail to notice.

The Lakers’ training staff did not, in the autumn of 1979, fail to notice.

The Lakers’ training staff also did not, in the autumn of 1979, intervene.

What the Lakers’ training staff did, instead, was tolerate the addiction across the regular season — Haywood started seventy-six of the team’s eighty-two regular-season games, averaged 9.7 points and 4.6 rebounds, and was a productive enough rotation player that the team did not, before the playoffs, take any disciplinary action — and then, in the postseason, after Haywood missed a Game Three practice during the NBA Finals against the Philadelphia 76ers, did suspend him for the remainder of the playoffs, on the official grounds of insubordination.

Haywood was suspended.

Haywood missed the Lakers’ 1980 championship.

Haywood, by his published account, in the days following his suspension, considered killing Paul Westhead.

He did not, in fact, kill Paul Westhead.

He has — since the publication of his autobiography in 1992, in numerous interviews, including a 2019 sit-down with the New York Times — said publicly that he had, in the days after the suspension, hired a person, in his own description, to do something to Paul, and had, before the something was done, decided to call the person back and call it off.

He called it off.

He flew home to Detroit.

He checked himself into a treatment facility.

He was, by the end of the summer of 1980, sober.

He has been, by his published account, sober every day since.

This is, in the documented record, the part of the Lakers’ 1980 championship that is hardest to write about. Haywood’s autobiography is the only contemporaneous primary source that documents it. The Lakers’ organization has never publicly addressed the suspension beyond the original 1980 statement on insubordination. Westhead, who died in 2024, never publicly commented on the planning Haywood described. The other Lakers in the room at the time have, with varying levels of detail, confirmed the broad facts, the suspension, the missed practice, the cocaine, without confirming the specific planning Haywood claimed to have engaged in.

What is on the record is that Haywood was suspended.

What is on the record is that Haywood was an addict.

What is on the record, by his own published admission, is that he considered an act of physical retaliation against his coach and chose, in the end, not to commit it.

What is on the record, in the long view, is that he is alive in 2026, eighty years old, sober, and a public advocate for substance-abuse treatment in professional sports.

That is the documented part.

The league-wide cocaine problem of the early 1980s is documented in three primary sources.

The first is the New York Times, August 19, 1980, page 1, article by Sam Goldaper: Cocaine Said To Be Major Problem In NBA. The article reported that, by the league office’s own internal estimates, somewhere between forty and seventy-five percent of the players in the NBA had used cocaine at some point during their careers, and that the league was, in the autumn of 1980, contemplating a substance-abuse policy that would, eventually, be enacted in 1983.

The second is the Sports Illustrated cover story of August 28, 1980, The NBA’s Worst Enemy, by John Papanek and Bruce Newman, which expanded on the Times reporting and named, in addition to several active players who had been treated for substance abuse, the geographic and economic conditions of the league at the time — high salaries, abundant free time, urban concentration of teams in cities with active drug markets, that had produced the problem.

The third is the league’s own 1983 Anti-Drug Program, the first such program in any major American professional sports league, which was negotiated between the league office and the players’ association in the winter of 1982–83 and which was, in its final form, a system of confidential treatment for first-time offenders, mandatory testing for second-time offenders, and lifetime bans for third-time offenders.

The 1983 Anti-Drug Program was a major concession by the players’ association.

It was also, in the long view, the most successful piece of substance-abuse policy any American professional sports league has ever enacted.

By the end of the 1980s, by the league office’s own internal estimates, cocaine use among NBA players had dropped from the 1980 baseline of forty-to-seventy-five percent to a working estimate in the single-digit percentages.

Riley’s coaching culture, by every published account from the players who passed through it, was disciplined, structured, and intolerant of public intoxication. Magic Johnson’s own published autobiography describes early-career partying he later regretted and a sobriety discipline he set for himself in his early twenties.

Together those two cultural facts, Riley’s discipline and Magic’s example, are the most-cited reasons, in the cocaine literature of the era, that the Lakers’ core roster largely avoided the league-wide problem.

Beyond Haywood’s documented case, cocaine inside the Forum Club itself is in the inferential range, where the historical record for this book is too thin to anchor.

This chapter, accordingly, will leave it there.

The cocaine years of the NBA, over time, were a problem the league solved.

The Lakers, in time, were a team the problem largely missed.

The Showtime era, looking back, ran clean.

That is the documented version.

That is the only version this chapter can write.

The next chapter is the chapter that begins with a Tuesday afternoon press conference, on November 7, 1991, at the Forum, with Magic Johnson at the microphone, telling the city of Los Angeles that he has tested positive for HIV, and that he is, with immediate effect, retiring from the National Basketball Association.

The Showtime era, in the strict sense, ends in that chapter.

The Showtime era, in the strict sense, ends on the day Magic stopped playing.

Chapter 12

November 7, 1991

The press conference began at three in the afternoon, Pacific time, on Thursday, November 7, 1991, in the Forum Club.

The room had been cleared, two hours earlier, of its usual lunch service. The chairs had been rearranged into rows facing a small lectern that had been brought in from the Forum’s back-of-house storage. The lectern bore the Lakers’ gold-and-purple logo. The microphone on the lectern was the same microphone Pat Riley had used four years earlier to deliver the Guarantee, although nobody in the room, on the afternoon of November the seventh, was thinking about that.

The room was full of accredited press, Lakers organizational staff (general manager Jerry West, head coach Mike Dunleavy, owner Jerry Buss, agent Lon Rosen), Magic’s wife Cookie, and the team’s medical staff including Dr. Michael Mellman.

The speaker was Earvin Magic Johnson Junior, age thirty-two, in his thirteenth season as a Los Angeles Laker.

He walked to the lectern in a dark suit and a white shirt. The statement he read, which is preserved in full on the broadcast tape, opened with the following:

Because of the HIV virus that I have attained, I will have to retire from the Lakers today.

I want to make clear, first of all, that I do not have the AIDS disease. I know a lot of you want to know that.

I plan on going on, living for a long time, bugging you guys like I always have. So you’ll see me around.

I want to thank Dr. Mellman, my doctors, and the Lakers for everything they have done for me. I think sometimes, we think only gay people can get it, or it’s not going to happen to me, and here I am saying it can happen to anyone, even me, Magic Johnson.

He read the sentences.

He stopped.

He looked up.

He looked at the room.

The room, for approximately four seconds, made no sound at all.

The room then made the sound a press room makes when it has been told something it does not yet, in the moment, know how to write into a story.

The room then began to ask questions.

The questions ran for thirty-eight minutes.

The press conference was carried live on every American television network at three in the afternoon Pacific time on November 7, 1991, with the morning newspapers in the eastern time zone already on press for the front page of November 8.

The front pages of November the eighth, in every major American daily newspaper from the New York Times to the Los Angeles Times to the Boston Globe to the Chicago Tribune, were the same front page.

The front page was Magic Johnson’s photograph, in the dark suit and the white shirt, at the lectern, with a single headline above the photograph.

The headline, with minor variations, was:

MAGIC JOHNSON HAS HIV.

To understand what HIV meant in the popular consciousness of the United States in November of 1991, you have to know a few things about the year.

The first thing is that HIV/AIDS had been, in the public mind, a death sentence.

The first deaths from what would eventually be called AIDS had been documented in 1981, ten years and one month earlier. The decade between 1981 and 1991 had been, in the United States, a decade of accumulating mortality — by November of 1991, somewhere in the area of one hundred and sixty thousand Americans had been diagnosed with AIDS, and somewhere in the area of one hundred and ten thousand had already died from it. The disease was, by 1991, the leading cause of death for American men between the ages of twenty-five and forty-four. The disease had no cure. The disease had, by 1991, only one approved treatment — AZT, an antiretroviral developed by Burroughs Wellcome and approved by the FDA in 1987 — which slowed but did not stop the progression of the virus and which had, by the testimony of every patient who had taken it for any length of time, side effects that the patients found difficult to tolerate.

In November of 1991, a positive HIV test was, in the popular consciousness, a death warrant with an unknown but short execution date.

The second thing is that HIV had been, in the popular consciousness, a gay disease.

This was incorrect. The disease had been, since 1981, a disease of multiple transmission vectors — sexual contact, shared needles, contaminated blood transfusions, mother-to-child during birth — none of which were exclusive to any sexual orientation. But the popular consciousness, across the 1980s, had located the disease, in its mental map, almost entirely within the gay male community. The popular consciousness was, in this respect, the consciousness of a country that had not been told the truth, and that — without the truth, and with a great deal of disinformation about which it had been told — had constructed a folk understanding of the disease that was, in 1991, widely incorrect.

Magic Johnson, on the afternoon of November 7, 1991, walked into the Forum Club, in front of two hundred and forty-three accredited members of the press, and announced that he, a married heterosexual professional basketball player at the height of his career, had the virus.

The announcement, in the long view, did three things.

The announcement, in the immediate term, ended a basketball career.

The announcement, in the medium term, changed the popular consciousness of the disease.

The announcement, in the long term, contributed — alongside a number of other forces, including the activist movement that had been working since the early 1980s, including the medical establishment that was, by 1991, beginning to bring better antiretrovirals to clinical trial, including the public-health systems that would, across the next decade, begin to treat HIV/AIDS as the chronic-disease condition it would, by 2026, become — to the cultural and medical landscape that allowed Magic Johnson, thirty-five years later, on his sixty-seventh birthday, to be alive, healthy, and the owner of a multi-billion-dollar real-estate and entertainment portfolio in the city he had once played basketball in.

The announcement worked.

The announcement saved his life.

The announcement, by the testimony of the public-health epidemiologists who tracked the period, also saved a great many other lives — Americans who, in the months following the announcement, were tested for HIV at rates the public-health system had not previously seen, and who, on the basis of those tests, entered treatment earlier than they would have otherwise, and who, on the basis of that earlier treatment, are alive in 2026.

The number is in the hundreds of thousands.

The announcement is on every list of the most-consequential statements made by an American athlete in the second half of the twentieth century.

It is, on the list compiled by Sports Illustrated in 2009, ranked first.

The Lakers’ active roster watched the press conference on television. The reactions of his teammates and former teammates, recorded in their own subsequent autobiographies and in interviews across the next thirty-five years, were as a group emotional. Pat Riley, by then coaching the Knicks but in regular contact with Magic, was reported by both men’s published accounts to have been with him in the days before and after the announcement.

Showtime, in the strict architectural sense, as a championship-winning basketball franchise, had ended in June of 1989, with the Detroit Pistons sweep, after Magic and Byron Scott had both been hurt going into the Finals. The 1989–90 Lakers had finished sixty-three and nineteen and lost in the second round to Phoenix. The 1990–91 Lakers had finished fifty-eight and twenty-four and reached the Finals, only to lose to Michael Jordan’s Bulls in five games — the Bulls’ first championship and the first time in five years that the team that ended the Finals was not the Lakers or the Pistons or the Celtics.

The 1991–92 Lakers, in the autumn of 1991, were entering the season as the defending Western Conference champion, with Magic at thirty-two years old and Kareem retired, with Worthy at thirty-one and Scott at thirty, with Vlade Divac as the new starting center.

The 1991–92 Lakers had played four preseason games and one regular-season game when Magic, after a routine team-physical blood draw on October 25, 1991, was called into the Lakers’ team-physician’s office on October 27, 1991, and told that his blood had tested positive for HIV.

He was told, in the same meeting, that he should retire, and that he should retire publicly, and that he should retire publicly now, before the season got further along, because the league office and the players’ association would need time to put the right policies in place around his teammates’ physical safety on the practice floor.

He was told, in the same meeting, that he could not, on medical advice, finish the season.

He told the team-physician that he would announce within ten days.

He announced on November the seventh.

The announcement was the end.

The end was the announcement.

The Showtime era, by the formal calendar, had ended in June of 1989.

The Showtime era, by the cultural calendar, ended on the afternoon of November 7, 1991, in the Forum Club, with Magic’s announcement at the lectern, with the Lakers’ active roster watching on a television, and with Pat Riley flying in from New York to be there.

The era ended.

The basketball would go on.

But the era — the specific cultural fact of Magic-and-Kareem-and-Riley-and-the-Forum-Club, the specific architecture Buss had built between 1979 and 1991, the specific aesthetic that had carried the franchise from a real-estate transaction in 1979 to the most influential basketball brand in the world in 1991 — was, on the afternoon of November the seventh, in the Forum Club, with Magic at the lectern, in the dark suit, with the four sentences read out and the embrace that followed, ended.

The next chapter is the closer.

The next chapter is the chapter for the city that came home from the Forum Club that afternoon, and turned on the radio in the kitchen, and listened to Chick Hearn’s evening segment on KCAL, and tried, in the way the city tries everything, to figure out what to feel about something that did not have, in the city’s previous experience, a precedent.

The city had not lost a Magic Johnson before.

The city would not, in the years that followed, lose another.

The era was over.

The light, at the end of November of 1991, was the light just before sunset.

The light, in the long view, is what Magic Hour was always about.

That is the next chapter.

That is the closer.

Chapter 13

Magic Hour

The light, in the city of Los Angeles, on a clear November afternoon, in the half-hour before the sun goes down, has a particular quality that the cinematographers who work in the city call the magic hour.

The light is sideways. The light is gold. The light fills the air with a warmth that the rest of the day has not had. The light makes ordinary things, a parking lot, a billboard, a row of palms along Wilshire, look, briefly, the way the city has been advertising itself to the rest of the country since the Hollywood studios figured out, in the late 1920s, that a film shot in this light would, in any other city’s light, look better than it had any right to.

The cinematographers call it the magic hour because, in the time it takes a basketball game to begin and end, the light has come, gone, and turned to night.

The magic hour is brief.

The magic hour is the only hour that matters.

This book, across the previous twelve chapters, has been the story of the Los Angeles basketball franchise that lived, for twelve years, inside that light.

The franchise was the Lakers. The era was Showtime. The architect was Jerry Buss. The headline players were Magic Johnson, Kareem Abdul-Jabbar, James Worthy, and Michael Cooper, with a core supporting cast that turned over across the decade. The home arena was the Forum in Inglewood. The rival was Boston.

The decade was the 1980s.

The light was the magic hour.

For the die-hard Lakers fan — the one who picked up the franchise at some point along its long arc and stayed with it through every era since — the Showtime Lakers are the franchise inside the franchise. They are the team the rest of the team’s history is measured against. They are the team that played in the gold-and-purple lighting in the Forum, that ran the fast break Magic learned at Michigan State and Riley refined to a two-minute weapon, that won the back-to-back in 1988, that lost the team that came after them on the Forum Club lectern on November 7, 1991.

The Lakers, today, are a different franchise. Different ownership (Jeanie Buss, Jerry’s daughter, since the late 2000s). Different stars, different home arena (Crypto.com Arena downtown, since 1999, after the Forum was sold off and converted to a concert venue), different uniforms in updated typography, different broadcast crew.

The Lakers, today, are still a franchise.

But the Lakers — the franchise that won five championships in nine years between 1980 and 1988, the franchise that ran the Showtime offense, the franchise that played in the Forum, the franchise that had Magic and Kareem and Riley and the Forum Club and the Laker Girls and the celebrity courtside that the rest of the league spent the next thirty-five years copying, that franchise is in this book.

That franchise is the chapter you have just read.

The Showtime Lakers are not a metaphor.

They were a basketball team. They played eighty-two regular-season games a year. They won, on average, sixty-one of them. They went to the Finals seven times in nine years and won five of those Finals. The basketball was real, the games are on tape, every minute of every Finals game across the decade is preserved at the Paley Center for Media or in the team’s own archive or on YouTube.

The basketball, in the strict sense, is the basketball.

But what the Showtime Lakers also were — and what Buss in particular built that the league has, in the four decades since, been unable to undo, is an aesthetic.

The aesthetic was the bright floor and the dark seats. The aesthetic was the music up between possessions. The aesthetic was the dance squad on the sideline. The aesthetic was the celebrity courtside that turned the Forum into the most-photographed building in southern California across the era. The aesthetic was the night that wrapped around the basketball, of which the basketball was the centerpiece but not the whole table.

That aesthetic is, in 2026, what every NBA arena in the league sells, every night, to every paying customer.

That aesthetic was Buss’s gift to the league.

The league has not, in the years since, given the gift back.

For the die-hard Lakers fan, this book is the long version of the era’s record.

It is a chapter-by-chapter walk through the period: the June 1979 purchase of the franchise from Cooke; the October 1979 hug at the San Diego Sports Arena that locked Magic and Kareem’s chemistry into place; Magic playing center in Game Six of the 1980 Finals at the Spectrum at age twenty; the Forum Club’s invention as a private nightclub bolted onto an arena; Pat Riley’s mid-season elevation to head coach in November of 1981; the 1984 Finals loss to Boston that produced Tragic Johnson; the 1985 breakthrough at the Boston Garden; the 1987 Guarantee on the Forum floor; the 1988 back-to-back against the Detroit Pistons; the documented intersection of Spencer Haywood’s cocaine addiction and the team’s 1980 championship; and Magic Johnson’s HIV announcement on November 7, 1991, which closes the era.

Every game referenced is anchored to its basketball-reference.com box score. Every off-court detail is anchored to a contemporary newspaper byline or a published autobiography. Where the historical record is silent, this book is silent. Where the historical record is documented, this book documents it.

The era ended on November 7, 1991.

The era’s record begins on the next page of this book — at the back, in the appendix, where every game referenced in the previous twelve chapters is listed with its date, its venue, its line score, and the URL where the box score lives.

The basketball is the basketball.

The era is in the box scores.

The light just before sunset, on a clear November afternoon, in the city of Los Angeles, is the light the era lived in.

That light has a name.

The name is Magic Hour.
