Chapter 1

The Last Inning

October 2, 2016. AT&T Park, San Francisco. A bright bay-side afternoon, the last regular-season game of the year, the Dodgers in town for a series that would not change the standings, they had clinched the West days before, and high in a small booth above home plate sat an eighty-eight-year-old man who had been calling Dodger games since the year Harry Truman finished his last full year in the White House. His hair was white. His voice, though softer now than the voice that had narrated Sandy Koufax’s perfect game and Hank Aaron’s seven-hundred-and-fifteenth home run, still carried that familiar warmth — the sound, as one writer once put it, of summer itself. When the bottom of the ninth ended and the game went final, he leaned toward his microphone for the last time as a regular employee of the Los Angeles Dodgers and said, slowly, the way he had said a million things slowly across sixty-seven seasons:

“I have said enough for a lifetime, and for the last time, I wish you all a very pleasant good afternoon.”

Then he was quiet.

That is where this book begins. Not at the beginning of his life, which we will get to, but at the end of his career — because it is the ending that explains the rest. The man who walked off the air that afternoon in San Francisco had narrated more baseball than any human being who has ever lived. He had begun in Brooklyn, in the booth with Red Barber, in 1950. He had moved with the team to Los Angeles in 1958. He had called perfect games and pennant races and World Series and an entire half-century of Octobers. He had outlived the era of the broadcast booth itself. By 2016 he was a man from a different country — a country where you sat on the porch with a transistor radio and someone you loved, and the voice on the air told you what was happening in a stadium you had never seen, and that was enough. That was, in fact, plenty.

The radio was the point.

Long before the screens, before the highlights, before the analytics and the launch angles and the WAR and the OBP, there was a kid with a transistor radio in his pocket and a battery he was trying to make last. The kid was probably eight or nine. The transistor was AM, plastic, smaller than a pack of cigarettes. He was supposed to be asleep. The radio was under the pillow. The volume was down to a whisper. And on the other end of the wire, calmly and patiently, a man in a tie was telling him about a game in a stadium he had not yet seen.

The kid grew up. He had a son. The son grew up. And here we are, three generations downstream, and the voice that connected all of them belongs to a man named Vin Scully, who in 2016 was leaving the booth for good.

This book is for the kid. And the son. And the man in the tie. It is for everyone who learned the game by listening.

Vincent Edward Scully was born in the Bronx on November 29, 1927, the son of an Irish immigrant silk salesman and an Irish immigrant mother. By the time he died, on August 2, 2022, at the age of ninety-four, he had spent more years calling Dodger baseball — sixty-seven, as the team’s primary broadcaster, with a season alongside Red Barber before that, than most people spend doing anything. To write his life is to write the life of the team. The team came to Los Angeles in 1958 and he came with it. Before that, he had been a young Fordham graduate in a Brooklyn booth, learning how to call baseball from a Mississippi-born former classical-music announcer named Walter Lanier “Red” Barber, who told him three things: One, never root for the team. Two, never quote yourself. And three, when something extraordinary happens, get out of the way.

For seven decades, Vin got out of the way.

When Sandy Koufax threw the perfect game on September 9, 1965, with twenty-nine thousand and a hundred and thirty-nine people in Dodger Stadium and a million butterflies, Vin did not narrate Sandy Koufax. He narrated the silence between Sandy Koufax’s pitches. When Henry Aaron hit number 715 on April 8, 1974, in Atlanta, on a national broadcast, Vin paused. He let the crowd carry the moment. Then he said: “What a marvelous moment for baseball. What a marvelous moment for Atlanta and the state of Georgia. What a marvelous moment for the country and the world. A black man is getting a standing ovation in the Deep South for breaking a record of an all-time baseball idol.” And then he was quiet again.

That habit, the habit of getting out of the way, is what distinguished him from every announcer who ever lived. He had been taught, at twenty-two, by a man who had been taught by the medium itself in the 1930s, that the voice on the radio is not the show. The game is the show. The voice is a guide.

By the time he stopped, in October 2016, that habit had become so much a part of how Americans listened to baseball that we had stopped noticing it. We had simply assumed that Octobers would always have a man with a quiet voice telling us what was happening on the field, and that the man would always be calm, and that he would never, under any circumstances, lose his composure. He never did. Across sixty-seven seasons. Through Brooklyn’s last World Series and Brooklyn’s loss of its team. Through Koufax and Drysdale and Garvey and Lopes and Russell and Cey and Fernando and Gibson and Kershaw. He never raised his voice except to match the moment, and when he raised it, he raised it once, and then he came back down.

He was, in the language of the technical age that came after him, the quietest possible interface between the game and the listener.

In Los Angeles, the day after his last broadcast, fans who had grown up with Vin’s voice on the kitchen radio said the same thing in different words. They had not realized, until the voice was gone, how much of their lives had been lived inside it.

This is a book about a man who narrated a team for sixty-seven years and a city for fifty-eight. It is, in the technical sense, a biography of Vin Scully. It is, in every other sense, a biography of how to listen.

The chapters that follow walk through the seasons. The 1950 season, when the Mississippi-born Barber put the twenty-two-year-old Scully in the booth at Ebbets Field and told him to stand up while he called the game so he would not get too comfortable. The 1955 season, when Brooklyn won its only World Series and the kid from Fordham, twenty-seven years old now, called innings of it from the press box at Yankee Stadium. The 1958 season, when the team moved west and the city of Los Angeles, which had never had a major-league team and did not understand what one was, learned the rules of baseball from a voice on a transistor. The 1965 season, Koufax. The 1974 season, Aaron. The 1981 season, Fernando, twenty years old, screwball, opening day, shutout. The 1988 season, Gibson, hobbling out of the dugout at Dodger Stadium, on one bad knee, against Dennis Eckersley, who had not given up a home run in a save situation in months, and Vin saying, in real time, the words that have become the most-replayed call in American sports history: “She is — gone! In a year that has been so improbable, the impossible has happened.”

And then the long quiet years. The 1990s and the 2000s. Vin alone in the booth, by his own preference. No color man. The team going through its various eras of plenty and famine. The voice steady through all of it. The transistor still in dad’s pocket.

There is a thing that happens, when you grow up with a voice on the radio, that no one warns you about. The voice becomes part of how you remember being a child. It is not metaphor. It is not nostalgia. It is the literal sound of summer for an entire generation of fans on the West Coast, and a slightly older generation of fans in Brooklyn before them, and a community of Mexican-American fans in East LA who learned English from him and Spanish-language baseball from Jaime Jarrín. The voice was an actual thing in the actual world. You could put it in your pocket. You could turn the dial and find it. You could fall asleep with it on.

And then one day, on October 2, 2016, you turned the dial and the voice said good afternoon for the last time, and went home.

This book is for everyone who turned the dial that day.

Pull up a chair.

Chapter 2

Fordham, 1949

The first thing to know about how Vin Scully became the greatest broadcaster in baseball is that in the late autumn of 1949, on a Saturday afternoon in November, he was handed a live network college-football microphone and sent up to the roof of Fenway Park, where he expected to find a press box and instead found a wooden platform with no shelter, no heat, no glass, and the wind coming off the Back Bay at something around twenty knots, and he called the game in his shirtsleeves for three hours without once mentioning, on the air, that he was freezing.

The game was Boston University against Maryland. He was twenty-one years old. He had graduated from Fordham University the previous spring and had spent the summer doing fill-in work at WTOP radio in Washington, D.C., covering Georgetown football and whatever else they would give him, and he had written, in the fall, a letter to a man at the CBS radio network named Red Barber, whose voice had been on the Brooklyn Dodgers’ radio broadcasts since 1939 and who was, in 1949, the most admired play-by-play announcer alive. Barber had read the letter and sent back a short reply that said, essentially, let’s see what you can do. The assignment was the BU-Maryland game. The location was Fenway. The network would pay for the train up from New York.

What happened next entered the Scully legend by way of Barber himself, who would tell it for the rest of his life, and by way of Vin himself, who told it for the rest of his.

Young Scully, the story goes, arrived at Fenway Park on the appointed Saturday, checked in with the network producer, handed over his overcoat and his hat at the press entrance, and was directed up a metal staircase to what was described to him as the broadcast position. The broadcast position turned out not to be a press box. The broadcast position was a boarded-over platform high on the Fenway roof, exposed to the open air, with a card table and a microphone and a folding chair. By the time he climbed onto the platform, the temperature was dropping through the forties and the wind was already steady. He had no coat. He could not go down and retrieve his coat without missing the kickoff. So he sat in the folding chair in his suit jacket and his shirtsleeves and his tie, and he called the game.

He did not mention the conditions on the air. He did not ask for a blanket. He did not editorialize about network planning. He called the football game. When it ended, he came down the metal staircase, collected his overcoat and his hat from wherever he had left them in the warm, and took the train back to New York.

A tape of the broadcast landed on Red Barber’s desk in the CBS radio offices the following week. What Barber heard on the tape was a young man calling a college football game from a cold rooftop with no indication in his voice that the rooftop was cold. What Barber deduced from the tape was that the young man had been taught, somewhere along the way, that the broadcast was not about the broadcaster. Barber called him in for a meeting.

The meeting led, within weeks, to an offer.

The offer was a seat in the Dodgers’ radio booth at Ebbets Field for the 1950 season.

Vin Scully was twenty-two years old. He had been a broadcaster, officially, for something under a year.

He would call Dodger games for the next sixty-seven.

To understand how he got to the Fenway roof in the first place, you have to go back to Washington Heights — the apartment on a Manhattan side street where a first-generation Irish-American kid who would one day be described as the voice of summer in Los Angeles was born, in the autumn of 1927, to a father he would barely remember and a mother he would remember perfectly.

His father was an immigrant silk salesman. His father died when Vincent was a small boy — too young, Vin would later say, to hold on to much beyond the idea of a father. His mother remarried an English-born merchant seaman named Allan Reeve, who raised him. The household was small. The household had a radio — one of those four-legged wood-cabinet consoles with the glowing dial, the kind of radio most Americans in 1935 thought of as the living-room’s beating heart. The radio was tuned, on autumn afternoons, to whatever college-football game the boy could find. He was eight or nine. He sat under the cabinet and he put his ear against the speaker grille and he listened to the crowd roar through the lower bass of the radio’s voice, and the experience, he would later say across forty-some interviews, was the experience of being inside a wave. The wave was the crowd. The wave was the game. The wave was somebody else telling him about it.

From the time he was nine years old he had wanted, with the kind of specificity most children never manage, to be the man telling him about it.

He went to Fordham Prep. He served briefly in the United States Navy at the end of the Second World War. He entered Fordham University on the GI Bill. He majored in communication. He minored in Spanish — the language of the Washington Heights streets his mother’s family lived on, the language that would one day give him, without his planning it, a way into the Mexican-American households of East Los Angeles whose patriarchs listened to Jaime Jarrín on one side of the kitchen and to him on the other. He joined the Fordham baseball team as an outfielder. He was, by his own account, a decent college player and not a great one — fast, left-handed at the plate, with a good glove and an accurate arm, batting high in the order.

In one spring game in the late nineteen-forties, Fordham played Yale. Vin looked across the diamond at the Yale first baseman, a tall left-handed kid named George Herbert Walker Bush, who would one day, decades later, sleep in the White House, and neither of them had any idea what the other would become. Scully told that story for the rest of his life. It was the only story from his Fordham playing days he told regularly.

He was a better broadcaster than ballplayer.

Fordham had a student-run FM radio station, WFUV, which had gone on the air in the spring of 1947. Scully was one of its first broadcasters. He called Fordham baseball games. He called Fordham basketball games. He called Fordham football games. He ran the dial back and forth, learned the equipment, learned the patter, learned, crucially, to describe events in ways that allowed a listener who was not watching to see them. This last skill was the core skill of the medium. You could have a beautiful voice and be useless at it. You could have a plain voice and be the best in the league. What mattered was whether the person with his ear to the radio, miles away, could see the field.

By the time he graduated in the spring of 1949, he could do it.

He wrote to every radio station he could find. He got one offer, from WTOP in Washington, filling in for the summer on Georgetown football. He took it. He did well. Then he wrote to Red Barber.

Then Fenway.

Vin would, in interviews across his subsequent career, occasionally be asked how he had kept from mentioning the cold that afternoon.

He would say, every time, the same thing.

He would say that his job was to describe the football game.

He would say that the cold was not the football game.

He would say that if the listener wanted to know about the cold, the listener could look out a window.

And then he would smile, and tell the reporter that Mr. Barber had taught him that, and that he had spent the next sixty years trying not to unlearn it.

Chapter 3

Brooklyn, 1950

Opening Day at Ebbets Field in April of 1950 smelled like peanuts and cigar smoke and cut grass and, from the direction of the right-field wall, depending on the wind, the Canarsie marshes. The ballpark sat on a plot of land bounded by Bedford Avenue and Sullivan Place and McKeever Place and Montgomery Street in the part of Brooklyn called Flatbush. It held roughly thirty-two thousand people when every seat was full, which for most of 1950 it was not, and its right-field wall was the strangest wall in baseball — angled, pocked, with a scoreboard built into it and a Schaefer beer sign mounted above the screen and a low concrete base that played balls at peculiar angles that every Dodger outfielder learned to read by the second week of the season. Above the right-field wall, on game days, a tenement woman named Hilda Chester rang a brass cowbell continuously for nine innings, because Hilda Chester had been ringing the cowbell at Ebbets since the late nineteen-thirties and nobody had asked her to stop. Along the third-base stands an amateur Brooklyn brass group called the Sym-Phony Band played, not well, but consistently, whatever the afternoon asked for, and an organist named Gladys Goodding, who lived in the apartment above the third-base stands and who also played the organ for the Rangers and the Knicks at Madison Square Garden, would sometimes, when the game got dull, play a few bars of something the Sym-Phony Band did not know, just to throw them off.

This was the ballpark into which, on a spring morning in 1950, a twenty-two-year-old broadcaster from Washington Heights named Vincent Edward Scully walked for the first time as a paid employee of the Brooklyn Dodgers.

He did not, on that first morning, walk into the broadcast booth. He walked through the players’ clubhouse, which Barber had told him to do, to introduce himself to the men whose games he would be calling. The clubhouse was small. The clubhouse smelled like liniment. The man who stood up first, tossed him a towel, and stuck out a hand, was Pee Wee Reese, captain of the Dodgers, a Kentucky-born shortstop who had played the infield beside Jackie Robinson since 1947 and who understood, better than almost anyone else in the building, that the social experiment happening around him was going to outlive all of them. Reese was thirty-one. Scully was twenty-two. Reese said: you the kid they hired to replace Ernie? Scully said yes. Reese said: welcome to Brooklyn.

The kid they hired to replace Ernie was Vin. Ernie was Ernie Harwell, who had sat in the Dodgers’ broadcast booth with Red Barber and Connie Desmond and who had left for the New York Giants and the Polo Grounds. Barber, needing a third voice, had remembered the tape from the Fenway roof. The tape from the Fenway roof became a salary of, as Vin later recalled with amusement, something like five thousand dollars for the season. It was the most money he had ever imagined. It was also, compared to the Dodger players, a pittance. Pee Wee Reese was making somewhere around twenty-five thousand. Jackie Robinson, the National League’s most valuable player of 1949, was making thirty-five. The broadcaster’s role, in 1950, was not a glamorous role. It was a trade, like plumbing.

Vin would spend the next sixty-seven years changing that.

Walter Lanier “Red” Barber was forty-two years old in 1950. He had been born in Columbus, Mississippi, and raised in Sanford, Florida. He had worked his way up through a student job at the University of Florida radio station, a CBS network tour, and the Cincinnati Reds booth, and he had arrived in Brooklyn in 1939 as the man the Dodgers hired to end the radio silence that had held the three New York teams in a handshake agreement, no broadcasting of games at all, out of fear that radio would hurt the gate, throughout most of the nineteen-thirties. Barber’s arrival had been controversial. Barber’s broadcasts had sold out Ebbets Field for the rest of the decade. By 1950 he was, in the estimation of the industry, the finest play-by-play broadcaster alive. He was also, to Vin Scully, a teacher.

Barber taught three things.

One: stand up in the booth. The booth at Ebbets had a pair of high stools, and Barber had removed them, because a broadcaster sitting down in a heated booth was a broadcaster who might relax, and a broadcaster who relaxed was a broadcaster who fell behind the game. Stand, he said. Feet planted. Eye on the diamond. You are a racehorse at the gate. You do not sit at the gate.

Two: never root. The home team and the visiting team and the umpire and the groundskeeper are each, in the moment of the broadcast, people you do not care about in any outwardly visible way. You care about them the way a judge cares about the parties in a civil case. You keep your thumb off the scale. If the Dodgers rally in the ninth, you describe the rally exactly the way you would describe a rally by the Phillies. If the Dodgers lose, you describe the loss with the same level of calm. You are not a fan. You are a reporter.

Three: when something extraordinary happens, get out of the way. The crowd is the instrument. The crowd is louder than any voice you will ever have, and the crowd is telling the story whether you are on the air or not. When the extraordinary happens, you name the event once, in the fewest words you can find, and then you stop talking, and you let the crowd say the rest.

Vin, who had been raised to listen to a football crowd through a wood-cabinet radio under a card table in Washington Heights, understood the third rule faster than either of the first two. The third rule was the rule he had been looking for.

The 1950 Brooklyn Dodgers went eighty-nine and sixty-five. They finished two games behind the Philadelphia Phillies, whose pennant-winning team would be remembered as the Whiz Kids, in a race that would not be decided until the final day of the season. They were a team in transition. Jackie Robinson was at his peak. Pee Wee Reese was the captain. Duke Snider, age twenty-three, was becoming a star. Roy Campanella was catching most days. Gil Hodges was at first base. Carl Furillo, the rifle-armed right fielder, was in right. Don Newcombe was the ace. The bench was thin. The pitching behind Newcombe was thinner. Manager Burt Shotton, in his mid-sixties, managed the club in a suit and a tie and a fedora, refusing to wear a uniform, because he was, as Barber used to say with quiet amusement, a gentleman.

The broadcasts Vin worked, that first year, were mostly the middle innings. Barber would call the first three. Desmond would call the middle three. Vin, the apprentice, would call the seventh through the ninth, unless Barber wanted the ninth for himself, which Barber usually did. Vin was not bothered. Vin was grateful for every inning. He stood, as instructed. He did not root, as instructed. When Duke Snider hit a home run against the Giants in May, Vin called the home run in eight words, it is over the wall and into Bedford Avenue, and then he shut up.

Barber heard the call back on tape, that night, in the booth. He said, as Vin later told the story, good. You’re learning.

What Barber could not have known, that first summer, was that the kid he had hired on the strength of a tape from a cold Fenway roof was going to outlast every broadcaster in the industry, by a margin of decades, and that the quiet way Vin was learning to call a home run, naming it in the fewest possible words and then stopping, was going to become, over the next seven decades, the single most widely imitated technique in the history of American sports broadcasting.

Barber knew only that he had taken in a kid who listened.

That was enough, in 1950, to make him happy.

The Dodgers lost the pennant on the last day of the season, at Ebbets Field, to the Phillies in the tenth inning, when Dick Sisler hit a three-run home run off Don Newcombe into the upper deck in left. Brooklyn went home for the winter. Vin went home for the winter too. He rode the subway back to Washington Heights. He thought about what he had learned. In the spring, Ebbets Field would open again, and there would be a booth, and he would stand in it, and he would not root.

Five years from that October, in 1955, Brooklyn would finally win the World Series.

He would call innings of it.

He would be twenty-seven years old.

But that is the next chapter.

Chapter 4

The Last Brooklyn World Series

October 4, 1955. Yankee Stadium. The bottom of the sixth inning of the seventh game of the World Series, and a left-handed Cuban outfielder named Edmundo Isasi Amorós, who has been on the field for three minutes, three minutes, was put into left field by manager Walter Alston as a defensive replacement at the start of the inning, who is six feet tall and carries himself like a featherweight, and who is wearing a glove on his right hand because he throws left, runs along the warning track in shallow left field at full speed with his head turned over his right shoulder watching a high slicing fly ball come down out of the dimming October sky off the bat of Lawrence Peter Berra, called Yogi.

The runners are on first and second.

There is one out.

The Dodgers lead, 2–0.

If the ball drops, the runners score, the game is tied, the inning continues, and the Yankees, who have won sixteen of the previous twenty-one World Series, will, in all likelihood, win their seventeenth. If the ball drops, the Brooklyn Dodgers — who have lost the World Series in 1941, 1947, 1949, 1952, and 1953, all five times to the Yankees, all five times by ways small and large that have entered the borough’s collective folklore as proof that God does not love Brooklyn, will lose again. They will lose to the same team. In the same stadium. On the same field. On the same first Tuesday in October that has been swallowing Brooklyn pennants for fifteen years.

The ball is in the air for what feels, to everyone in the borough watching, like an entire afternoon.

Amorós runs.

He runs until the ball is no longer in front of him, until it is to the right of him, until the only piece of equipment that can catch it is the glove on the end of his fully extended right arm. He stretches the arm. The ball lands in the webbing. Amorós, who has only been a Dodger for two seasons, who barely speaks English, who arrived from Cuba via the Brooklyn farm system, plants his right foot, pivots, and throws the ball back to the cutoff man at shortstop, who is Pee Wee Reese, who has been the Brooklyn shortstop for fifteen years and has been on the losing end of all five previous World Series.

Reese has the ball.

The runner from first, Gil McDougald, has rounded second, has assumed the ball would drop, and is at this moment thirty feet from the bag he has just left.

Reese throws to first.

Hodges has it.

McDougald is doubled off.

There are two outs in the sixth inning of Game Seven of the World Series.

The Dodgers will score no more runs and the Yankees will score no runs and at the end of the bottom of the ninth inning, when Elston Howard rolls a soft ground ball to Pee Wee Reese, who throws it across the diamond to Gil Hodges, the Brooklyn Dodgers — who have never, in their seventy-five-year existence, won a championship; who have lost five times to the Yankees in the previous fourteen years; whose fans have shaken their heads on subway platforms and at lunch counters and on Bedford Avenue stoops for so many summers that wait till next year has become not a slogan but a folk prayer, will be world champions.

The pitcher who will throw that final pitch, Johnny Podres, is twenty-three years old.

The shortstop who will catch the relay from Amorós and throw to Hodges is thirty-six years old.

The broadcaster in the Mutual radio booth, who will, in the moment Hodges squeezes the throw and the dugout empties and the Brooklyn fans in section after section of Yankee Stadium begin a sound the Bronx has never quite heard before, lean toward the microphone and say nine words and stop, is twenty-seven years old.

His name is Vin Scully, and he has been calling Dodger games for five years and seven months, and he is, on the afternoon of October 4, 1955, the youngest broadcaster known to have called a World Series.

The nine words he says are:

Ladies and gentlemen, the Brooklyn Dodgers are the champions of the world.

He does not embellish.

He does not editorialize.

He has been told, six years ago, by a man from Mississippi, that when something extraordinary happens, you get out of the way.

He gets out of the way.

The crowd does the rest.

To understand why the borough lost its mind that afternoon, you have to back up four years to a different October, in a different ballpark, where a New York Giants outfielder named Bobby Thomson hit a home run off a Dodgers pitcher named Ralph Branca that has been remembered ever since as the Shot Heard Round the World, and you have to keep backing up — through 1953, when the Dodgers lost the World Series to the Yankees in six games, and 1952, when the Dodgers lost to the Yankees in seven, and 1949, when the Dodgers lost to the Yankees in five, and 1947, when the Dodgers lost to the Yankees in seven, and 1941, when the Dodgers lost to the Yankees in five — until you arrive at the unbroken twenty-year ledger of Brooklyn defeat that, by the spring of 1955, had become a kind of identity. The Dodgers were the team that didn’t win.

The 1955 Dodgers were a team that did.

They won twenty-two of their first twenty-four games. They opened up a ten-game lead by Memorial Day. They finished the regular season at ninety-eight and fifty-five, thirteen and a half games ahead of the second-place Milwaukee Braves, with the National League pennant clinched on September the eighth — which was the earliest pennant clinch, by date, in National League history at that point. The lineup read like a memorial plaque to Brooklyn baseball: Reese at short, Robinson at third, Hodges at first, Snider in center, Campanella behind the plate, Furillo in right, Gilliam at second, and at the back of the order, in left, a left-handed Cuban kid named Sandy Amorós whose nickname in the clubhouse was El Moro and whose glove, as we have just established, would in October catch the most important fly ball in the history of the borough.

The pitching was Newcombe and Erskine and Podres and Loes and Roe.

The manager was Walter Alston, in his second year on the job, a quiet former Cardinals farmhand from Darrtown, Ohio, whose entire major-league playing career consisted of a single at-bat in 1936 in which he struck out, and who had been hired by general manager Buzzie Bavasi to manage the Dodgers because, Bavasi later said, he wouldn’t talk too much. Alston wouldn’t.

The Dodgers’ season was workmanlike. The Yankees’, as always, was Yankees-style — wave after wave of starting-pitching depth, the Stengel platoons, Mantle in center field at age twenty-three, Berra behind the plate, Skowron at first, McDougald floating between second and third. The Yankees won ninety-six and fifty-eight, took the American League pennant, and met the Dodgers in the World Series the way they had met the Dodgers five times before: as the team that had not yet lost.

Game One: Yankees, six to five. Whitey Ford pitching against Newcombe. At Yankee Stadium.

Game Two: Yankees, four to two. Tommy Byrne over Loes. Yankee Stadium again.

The papers, on the morning of October first, ran the same word above the same column on the same page they had been running it for fifteen years.

Bums.

What happened next is the part that the borough kept and never let go of.

The Series moved to Ebbets Field for Games Three, Four, and Five. The Brooklyn club, down two games to none, in front of a crowd that had been waiting fifteen years for someone to give them something good to take home, won three games in a row. Game Three was a Podres complete game, eight innings of work, the Dodgers winning eight to three. Game Four was a Campanella home run and an Hodges home run and a Snider home run, the Dodgers winning eight to five. Game Five was Snider going deep twice and Roger Craig pitching well and a Sandy Amorós home run, the Dodgers winning five to three. The Series stood at three games to two for Brooklyn when it returned to Yankee Stadium for Games Six and Seven.

Game Six the Yankees won, five to one, behind Whitey Ford. Tied at three.

Game Seven was Podres against Tommy Byrne.

Podres was twenty-three. Podres had grown up in Witherbee, New York, three hundred miles up the Hudson, in iron-mining country, and he had been pitching in the Dodgers’ organization since 1951. Game Seven was his second start of the Series. He had won Game Three. The afternoon of Game Seven he ate a steak for breakfast at the Concourse Plaza Hotel, walked to Yankee Stadium, and threw what would later be remembered as one of the most disciplined complete-game shutouts in the history of the World Series. He went the full nine. He gave up eight hits. He walked two. He struck out four. He held the Yankee lineup, which had averaged five and a half runs a game during the regular season, to zero.

The Dodgers scored a run in the fourth inning when Hodges singled in Campanella, who had doubled.

The Dodgers scored a run in the sixth inning on a Hodges sacrifice fly that brought home Reese.

That was all the runs.

Two to nothing.

In the bottom of the sixth, with two on, one out, and Berra at the plate, Walter Alston walked out from the dugout and made his only managerial decision of the afternoon that anyone would remember for the next seventy years. He called Junior Gilliam in from left field and put him at second base. He called Don Zimmer off the bench. He sent Amorós to left.

Berra came up.

The pitch was a fastball low and away.

Berra, an aggressive opposite-field hitter when he had to be, sliced it down the left-field line, and you have already read what happened next.

What you have not read is what Vin Scully said in the bottom of the ninth, after Howard’s roller, after Reese’s throw, after the catch at first.

What Vin said, after the famous nine words, was nothing.

He let the Mutual broadcast hold the silence and let the Yankee Stadium crowd carry it. In Brooklyn — in living rooms, on stoops, on barstools, in the diners and the bakeries where the radios had been on all afternoon, the borough cried.

The borough had won.

The next morning, the New York Daily News ran the photograph of Pee Wee Reese hugging Johnny Podres on the field at Yankee Stadium, beneath a headline that, in the inverted vernacular of the borough, said the only thing the borough wanted to say.

WHO’S A BUM!

Two months later, the Brooklyn Eagle, which had folded in January of that same year, would not have to print a paper about it. The borough printed it themselves, in conversation, on every corner, for the rest of the autumn.

The borough’s joy, he said, was not his job. The borough’s joy was the borough’s. His job was to identify, in nine words or fewer, the fact that had just become true, and then to step out of the way and let the borough have it.

He stepped out of the way.

The borough had it.

It would have it for two more seasons before it lost the team to a city it could not yet pronounce, three thousand miles to the west, where the Pacific was warmer and the orange groves had not yet been bulldozed.

But the borough does not yet know that.

In October of 1955, the borough has just won.

The borough is going to be all right.

Chapter 5

The Move

The press release that broke the borough was issued from the offices of the Brooklyn Dodgers, Inc., at 215 Montague Street in downtown Brooklyn, on the afternoon of October 8, 1957. It was four sentences long. It said, in language that had been worked over by the team’s attorneys for a week, that in view of the inability to obtain a satisfactory location for a new ballpark in the city of New York, the directors of the Brooklyn Dodgers had voted to transfer the franchise to Los Angeles, California, beginning with the 1958 season. The release was signed by the team president. The team president was Walter Francis O’Malley.

In the borough, the press release was received the way a death notice is received.

In the borough, depending on which kitchen you were standing in or which subway car you were riding, the man who had signed it was a businessman, an opportunist, a thief, a serpent, or, in the formulation of one of the borough’s most enduring jokes, one of the three worst people in human history, the others being Hitler and Stalin, in some order, with O’Malley running second.

What he was, in fact, was a real-estate man.

Which is the same thing as saying that what he was, in fact, was a man who had looked at the corner of Atlantic and Flatbush, where he had wanted to build a new domed stadium for the Brooklyn Dodgers, and had been told by a New York City parks commissioner named Robert Moses, who in 1957 controlled approximately every shovelful of public land in the city, that he could not have the parcel he wanted, that he could have a different parcel in Queens that he did not want, and that the conversation about Brooklyn was, as far as Robert Moses was concerned, over.

O’Malley took the conversation to Los Angeles.

The mayor of Los Angeles, a man named Norris Poulson, took it back.

What Poulson offered O’Malley was, in the language of the deal, three hundred acres of city-owned land in a neighborhood called Chavez Ravine, north of downtown, where there had once been a small Mexican-American community that the city had cleared for a public-housing project that had then been canceled, and where the land had been sitting, half-graded and weedy, since 1953. Poulson would deed the land to the Dodgers. The Dodgers would build a stadium on it. The mayor’s office would handle the political theater.

In Brooklyn, the press release went out on October 8.

In Los Angeles, on October 9, the morning newspapers ran headlines that said, more or less, we have a major-league team.

In Brooklyn, the borough went into mourning.

It mourned all winter.

It mourned through Christmas. It mourned through the first months of 1958. It mourned in stages — the way a family mourns a parent, in shock, then in anger, then in slow accommodation. The owner who had taken the team did not return to the borough. The newspapers that had run the team’s box scores for the previous seventy-three years began running, instead, the box scores of the New York Yankees and the New York Giants, the second of whom, perhaps not coincidentally, had also moved that same autumn, to a city in California called San Francisco, where they would play in a windswept stadium named Seals Stadium until a bigger park called Candlestick could be built. New York, in the autumn of 1957, lost two of its three major-league franchises in eight days. The borough lost the only one it cared about.

Vin Scully, who had spent the previous eight years of his life calling Brooklyn baseball — who had moved into a small apartment near the elevated subway in Bay Ridge, who had introduced himself, on summer evenings, to the cab drivers and the deli men and the dock workers who knew him by his voice and not his face, who had stood next to Red Barber in the booth at Ebbets Field and had been told, by Barber, that the borough’s relationship with the team was the most beautiful and most unrequited love affair in American sport — packed his Brooklyn life into a station wagon, in February of 1958, and drove west.

He was twenty-nine.

He was about to call major-league baseball, for the first time, in a city that had never had any.

To understand what the city Vin drove into was like in the spring of 1958, you have to imagine, briefly, a Los Angeles that was still legible. A city that had not yet been swallowed by the freeway grid. A city that ended, on its eastern edge, at the foothills above Pasadena, and on its western edge, at the dunes north of Santa Monica, with a center of gravity downtown — where the Bradbury Building still smelled like the rust on the iron banisters and the streetcars still ran along Spring Street and the grand old movie palaces along Broadway still showed second-run pictures to crowds of clerks and waitresses on lunch breaks. The city had palms, but not yet the tall ones along the west side that television cameras would later turn into the city’s signature. The city had a freeway, singular, that connected downtown to Pasadena. The city did not have a major-league baseball team. Until Vin arrived, with his station wagon, in February of 1958, the city had not had a major-league baseball game on its dirt in living memory.

It also did not have a stadium.

The team that O’Malley had moved west had nowhere to play.

The team that O’Malley had moved west would play, the city had decided, at the Los Angeles Memorial Coliseum, which was a football stadium, built for the 1932 Olympic Games, with a seating capacity of approximately one hundred thousand and a configuration that had been designed for a sport in which the field is rectangular. The Coliseum could not, by any honest engineering measurement, fit a baseball diamond. The Coliseum was made to fit a baseball diamond by reorienting the field along the diagonal of the rectangle, which produced a left-field foul line that was two hundred and fifty-one feet from home plate, an outfield wall in left that was forty-two feet high — protected by a wire screen that the writers immediately christened the Chinese Wall, in reference to the Great Wall and not the Great Diet, and which we will call by the same name even though the comparison is one we would no longer make today — and a right-field foul line that was just over four hundred feet, with the outfield in right opening up into a void that allowed for triples that no major-league outfielder had ever before been asked to chase.

It was, as ballparks go, ridiculous.

It was also, as venues go, enormous.

On the afternoon of April 18, 1958, the Los Angeles Dodgers played their first home game in Los Angeles in the Memorial Coliseum, against the San Francisco Giants, who had moved that same spring, in front of a crowd of seventy-eight thousand six hundred and seventy-two paying customers. The crowd was the largest crowd to attend a regular-season major-league baseball game up to that point in the history of the sport. The Dodgers won the game, six to five. Hodges hit a home run. Reese, age thirty-nine, the Brooklyn captain, started at third base and went one for three. Vin Scully, in the radio booth in section twelve, called the game to a city that had purchased, in the months leading up to opening day, more transistor radios per capita than any other city in the United States.

The transistor radios were the part nobody had foreseen.

The Coliseum’s upper deck was so far from the field, at certain seats, more than five hundred feet, that fans in the upper rows could not, with the naked eye, identify the players on the field. They could see motion. They could see the white of the home uniforms and the gray of the road. They could not see who had hit the ball or who had caught it. The crowd in the upper deck, on opening day in April of 1958, did the only thing it could do.

It listened.

It held small plastic AM radios to its ears.

It tuned to KMPC, which had bought the broadcast rights, and listened to a young man with a small soft voice tell it what was happening in front of its own face.

Vin Scully, age twenty-nine, suddenly had a relationship with the audience for the team that had no precedent in the history of professional sport. The audience was not, as it had been at Ebbets Field, a small Brooklyn community on the other side of the wireless, listening to him in their kitchens. The audience was the actual people inside the actual stadium, sitting in the actual seats, with their actual radios, listening to him narrate, in real time, what was happening in front of their actual eyes, which they could not, from their actual seats, see clearly enough to follow without him.

He was, in the strict sense, the user interface of Dodger Stadium before Dodger Stadium had been built.

He would remain that interface for the next fifty-eight years.

The Coliseum years lasted four seasons. The Coliseum years included a 1959 World Series championship, won at the Coliseum in the second-deepest crowd in postseason history (more than ninety thousand for Game Five). The Coliseum years included Drysdale and Koufax in the early innings of their careers. The Coliseum years included the discovery, made simultaneously by every Brooklyn-born fan who had moved west and every Los Angeles-born fan who had never had a team before, that they shared a single asset, which was the voice in the box.

In 1962, the team moved to a new stadium, a four-tiered open-air ballpark designed by architect Emil Praeger and built into the bowl at Chavez Ravine, which O’Malley had asked to be sited so that, from the upper deck, the entire skyline of Los Angeles would be visible at sunset, with the San Gabriel Mountains in the background. They named it Dodger Stadium. It opened on April 10, 1962, with a Cincinnati Reds and Los Angeles Dodgers game in which the visitors won six to three. Vin called it, from a booth on the press level, with a clear view of home plate and a clearer view of the western sky, where, every evening at the start of every game from April to October for the next fifty-five years, the sun would set behind the right-field bleachers in a color the borough of Brooklyn had never been able to produce.

He was thirty-four.

He was home.

So was the team.

The borough of Brooklyn, in February of 1958, had grieved. It had grieved all winter. By the spring of 1958, when the team was settling in three thousand miles away, the borough had begun the second stage of its grief, which is a low, slow, irreducible anger that does not go away.

Some Brooklynites would never forgive Walter O’Malley.

Some Brooklynites would never listen to a Dodgers game again.

But many Brooklynites, after a while, did the only thing the heart can do when the heart cannot go where it wants. They turned on the radio. They tuned the dial until they could find the station that, on certain summer nights, when the atmospheric conditions were right and the sky over the Atlantic was clear, would carry, on a single weak channel out of Pittsburgh or Cleveland or sometimes Chicago, a re-broadcast of the Dodgers’ Pacific Coast feed.

They listened.

The voice on the other end was the same voice that had taken Brooklyn’s heart west.

It was, Brooklyn would slowly come to understand, the only piece of Brooklyn that was still, in the technical sense, on the air.

He would carry the borough with him for the rest of his life.

He would carry the borough into Chavez Ravine.

He would carry it through Koufax. Through Drysdale. Through Garvey. Through Fernando. Through Gibson. Through Kershaw. Through every ballplayer the franchise put on a field for the next half-century.

The borough would never get its team back.

But every now and then, on a summer night, on a transistor radio held to the ear of an old man in a Bay Ridge kitchen, the borough could still hear itself.

That, in the end, was the move.

Chapter 6

The Coliseum and the Transistor

The thing nobody had foreseen, on the morning of April 18, 1958, when the Los Angeles Dodgers played their first home game in Los Angeles in front of seventy-eight thousand six hundred and seventy-two paying customers in a football stadium that had been built for the 1932 Olympic Games, was that more than a third of the seventy-eight thousand six hundred and seventy-two paying customers were going to bring with them, in their pockets, in their handbags, clipped to their belts, a small plastic AM transistor radio.

The transistor radio was, in 1958, eight years old as a consumer device. The first commercial pocket transistor, the Regency TR-1, manufactured in Indianapolis, had reached drugstore shelves in October of 1954, at a retail price of forty-nine dollars and ninety-five cents. By the spring of 1958, the price had dropped, the casings had gotten smaller, the brands had multiplied, Zenith, Magnavox, Sony, RCA, Sylvania, and a working transistor radio could be had for under fifteen dollars. By the spring of 1958, owning one was no longer a flex. Owning one was, increasingly, the way one walked around the world.

What nobody had calculated — not the team, not the network, not the writers, not Walter O’Malley, not the city of Los Angeles — was what would happen when a city that had never had a major-league team in its lifetime, and which was about to install one of the largest unmodified football stadiums in America as the team’s home ballpark, met the new portable consumer technology of the post-war era at the same point in space and the same point in time.

What happened was the transistor radio became a piece of stadium equipment.

The Coliseum’s seating bowl was, as we established in the previous chapter, ridiculous as a baseball venue. It was also — at a maximum capacity of just over ninety thousand and a sightline geometry that placed certain upper-deck seats more than five hundred feet from home plate — the largest baseball seating bowl any American city had ever attempted. From a great many of those seats, on a sunny afternoon, the action on the field could not be reliably resolved by an unaided human eye. Players could be identified, vaguely, by the color of their uniforms. The motion of the ball, in flight, was almost never trackable. Pitches could not be seen. Calls at the plate could not be seen. Substitutions could not be seen.

The fan in section thirty-two of the upper Coliseum, on opening day in April of 1958, was therefore presented with a mathematical problem.

He had paid for a ticket.

He could not see the game.

What he could do, with the small plastic device he had brought with him, was tune to a frequency on which a soft-voiced young man named Vin Scully — who was in a booth in the press box, with a clear view of the field, with a binocular scorebook open in front of him, and with the network engineer’s microphone live in front of his mouth — was describing what was happening in front of the fan’s actual face, in real time, into the fan’s actual ear.

The fan, in section thirty-two, took the radio out of his pocket.

He turned it on.

The fan, in the next section over, did the same.

The fan in front of them did the same.

The fan in front of them did the same.

By the third inning of the game, much of the upper deck was holding small black plastic radios to its ears, each one tuned to KMPC, each one carrying the same young man’s soft-voiced narration, each one whispering a description of the game that the eyes in those seats could not, from that distance, otherwise resolve.

The crowd was watching. The crowd was also, simultaneously, listening.

The voice the crowd was listening to was Vin’s.

The reaction the crowd produced was, accordingly, a reaction synced to a single broadcaster’s narration, slightly delayed off the action on the field. The upper deck cheered when Vin said the ball had gone for a hit, and groaned when Vin said it had gone foul.

Vin was, in the strict sense, conducting the upper deck.

He was the user interface.

He was the city’s first piece of personal-computing infrastructure, three decades before the city would have a name for that.

And the audience, in 1958, was a brand-new audience — Mexican-American mechanics from East LA, Iowa-born retirees in San Pedro, aerospace engineers from Manhattan Beach, Black families from South Central, Beverly Hills lawyers, Long Beach dockworkers, Encino tax accountants, the whole sprawling polyglot of post-war Los Angeles — that had never, before the spring of 1958, had a single shared cultural object.

It had one now.

The shared cultural object was a baseball game it could not quite see.

The shared cultural object was a voice on a transistor radio.

The Coliseum years lasted four full seasons, 1958 through 1961, and they were strange seasons. The 1958 Dodgers, in their first Los Angeles year, finished seventh in an eight-team National League, which was the franchise’s worst season since the early 1900s. The 1959 Dodgers, with the same essential roster a year more settled, won the World Series. The 1960 Dodgers fell back to fourth. The 1961 Dodgers, with a young Sandy Koufax beginning to figure out the strike zone in earnest, finished second.

The 1959 World Series was the strangest piece of Coliseum baseball any city had ever produced. It was, in part, the product of sheer geographic absurdity — the left-field screen, two hundred and fifty-one feet from home plate, ran the full length of the configured outfield in left, and balls that would have been routine pop-ups in any other major-league park became home runs over the screen at the Coliseum on a regular basis.

The Series itself, against the Chicago White Sox, was a six-game affair. The Dodgers won it. Game Five at the Coliseum drew a paid attendance of ninety-two thousand seven hundred and six — the largest crowd ever to attend a Major League Baseball game, a record that would stand, incidentally, for sixty-one years. Vin called the World Series in his second go-round at it; he was thirty-two years old, six years removed from his Brooklyn debut as, by the contemporary record, the youngest broadcaster known to have called one. The team that had been world champions in 1955 was world champions again in 1959, in a city it had been calling home for less than two seasons.

The team had survived the move.

The voice had brought it across.

What was happening, in those first four years, was that an entire municipality — a city of two and a half million people that had, within a decade, become a city of three million — was learning the rules of a sport from a single person.

It was learning the strike zone from him.

It was learning the difference between a curveball and a slider from him.

It was learning the unwritten rules — when to attempt a sacrifice bunt, when to walk the cleanup hitter intentionally, when the manager pulls his starter for a left-hander out of the bullpen, from him.

It was learning, in some neighborhoods of East LA where the patriarch of the family had grown up listening to professional baseball in Spanish in Sonora or Sinaloa or Mexico City, the names of major-league players who had not yet existed in the Mexican leagues. It was learning these names from him.

In the Coliseum’s upper deck, on summer evenings, beneath the haze that hung over downtown Los Angeles in the years before the catalytic converter was invented, Vin Scully was teaching twenty-five thousand people simultaneously how to be a baseball fan.

There has never been another moment like it in American sport.

The technology that allowed it — a small portable consumer device that could carry a single voice into the ear of the individual stadium-seated listener while leaving every neighbor’s ear free to listen to their own version — would be displaced, within a decade, by louder stadium speakers and bigger scoreboards and, eventually, by personal smartphones and team apps and league-issued in-arena Wi-Fi.

But for four summers, in a football stadium that should not have hosted baseball, in a city that had never had a major-league team, three million people learned a sport from one man.

And then, in the spring of 1962, the team moved across town to Chavez Ravine, into a beautiful new four-tiered ballpark with a clear view of the western sky and a press box the team had designed around Vin’s particular preferences — closer to home plate than usual, with windows that opened, with a small interior crew booth set off to the side so that Vin could, when he wanted to, work alone — and the transistor radio, for the next fifty-four summers, never quite left.

The fans, even in the lower bowl at Dodger Stadium, where they could see the ball and the bat and the call, kept the radio in their pocket, or on the seat next to them, or in the cup-holder built into the seat back, or, in the digital era after about 2008, in their phone with the SportsNet LA app.

They wanted the voice with them.

The voice was, by the time Dodger Stadium opened, no longer just the voice that told them what was happening on the field.

The voice was now a piece of the field.

The voice, eventually, was the field.

For the next half century, every fan who put on the radio at a Dodgers game, anywhere in the city of Los Angeles, was — whether or not they were aware of it, whether or not they had been there in 1958 or had been born thirty years later — listening to a man who had been carried west, in a station wagon, from a borough that no longer had a team, by a sport that no longer had its old shape, into a city that had not yet known what to do with him until it gave him an upper deck full of people who could not see, and a small plastic radio in every pocket, and four summers in a football stadium to teach a city its first language.

Chapter 7

Koufax — September 9, 1965

It is nine forty-six in the evening, Pacific time, on the ninth of September, 1965. The ballpark is Dodger Stadium. The pitcher on the mound is a thirty-year-old left-handed Brooklyn-born Jewish kid named Sanford Koufax, who has been a major-league pitcher for eleven seasons and who has been, for the last four of those seasons, the best pitcher in the National League and, in the considered opinion of every major-league hitter who has had to face him with the count two and two and the bases empty and the wind blowing in from left, the most difficult left-handed pitcher to hit cleanly in the history of the sport. He has thrown, on this evening, eight innings of perfect baseball against the visiting Chicago Cubs. He has retired twenty-four straight men. He has not allowed a runner to reach first base by any means — no hits, no walks, no errors, no hit batsmen. The Dodgers lead, one to nothing, on a run scored in the fifth inning on a walk and a wild pitch and a throwing error.

There are two outs in the top of the ninth.

The batter due up is Harvey Kuenn, the Cubs’ second-string outfielder, a former American League batting champion, a contact hitter, age thirty-four, in the twilight of his career.

Twenty-nine thousand one hundred and thirty-nine paying customers are in the seats.

A man named Vin Scully, age thirty-seven, is in the radio booth.

What Vin Scully is about to do, in the next two and a half minutes, is produce what is generally considered, by every working broadcaster in the history of American sports radio since, the single most beautifully constructed piece of live unscripted radio play-by-play ever recorded.

He has been getting ready for it for sixteen years.

He has, since Red Barber put him at a card table on the Fenway roof in November of 1949 and told him to call a college football game in his shirtsleeves, been preparing, without being aware that he was preparing, for the bottom-of-the-ninth call he is about to make.

The Cubs’ broadcaster, on the visitors’ radio feed, is a man named Lou Boudreau. The game is not nationally televised. The Dodgers’ flagship station, KFI, is recording the broadcast.

What Vin says, at one minute and forty-two seconds before the final pitch of the game, is the part that everyone remembers.

He looks at his scorecard. He looks at the field. He sees the small white ball already in the catcher’s glove for the second strike on Kuenn. He sees Sandy Koufax on the mound — sweating, exhausted, the slider gone, working entirely on the fastball and the sweeping curveball that has been the second-best pitch in baseball for the last four years. He sees the home plate umpire, Ed Vargo, in his crouch. He sees the third-base coach for the Cubs in his coach’s box. He sees the right fielder, Roberto Clemente — wait, the right fielder is not Clemente, Clemente is in Pittsburgh, the Cubs’ right fielder is Don Landrum — he sees Don Landrum at the proper distance, the Dodgers’ right fielder Ron Fairly mirroring him on the other side, the entire diamond and its outfield converging on the moment.

He says, into the microphone:

There are twenty-nine thousand and one hundred and thirty-nine people in the ballpark, and a million butterflies.

Then he says nothing for two seconds.

Then he says:

Twenty-nine thousand and one hundred and thirty-nine paid, but there’s many more than that, who came in with the help of friends.

Then, beat:

The time on the scoreboard is nine forty-six. The date, September the ninth, 1965, and Koufax working on veteran Harvey Kuenn.

Then:

Sandy into his windup, here’s the pitch, fastball, fouled back. Strike one, wait, strike two.

He pauses, corrects, lets the moment hang.

Strike two.

Then he begins, quietly, and in a way that no broadcaster has ever quite done before in this kind of moment, to give the listener a list of things — small concrete things, things that an unmoved observer at the ballpark would notice without thinking — to anchor the listener in the present tense before the moment lifts off.

He says: Sandy back of the rubber, now toes it. All the boys in the bullpen up to take a look.

He says: Two and two to Harvey Kuenn. One strike away.

He says: Sandy into his windup. Here’s the pitch.

The pitch is a fastball. Kuenn swings.

The bat misses the ball. The ball is in the catcher’s glove. The catcher’s glove is John Roseboro’s glove. The umpire’s right arm goes up. Sandy Koufax has thrown a perfect game. It is the fourth no-hitter of his career and the first perfect game in the National League since 1880.

What Vin Scully says, at the moment the bat misses the ball, is a single word.

The word is:

Swung — on, and missed!

Then he stops.

He says nothing on the broadcast for a long stretch, and the microphone hears, instead, the Dodger Stadium crowd. It is not exactly a cheer. It is not exactly a scream. It is the sound a city makes when it has just witnessed something it knows it will not see again, and which it understands, while it is still happening, to be the kind of thing it will remember for the rest of its life.

The pause, on a live radio broadcast, is a long one. It is, on this particular broadcast, exactly the right length.

When Vin comes back, what he does is the thing nobody else has ever quite figured out how to do. He does not summarize. He does not editorialize. He does not provide the highlights. He does not tell the listener what they have just heard.

He gives the listener a picture.

He says — and here we are quoting from the publicly archived broadcast:

On the scoreboard in right field it is 9:46 p.m. in the City of the Angels, Los Angeles, California. And a crowd of twenty-nine thousand and one hundred and thirty-nine just sitting in to see the only pitcher in baseball history to hurl four no-hit, no-run games. He has done it four straight years, and now he caps it: on his fourth no-hitter, he made it a perfect game.

He pauses again.

Then he says:

And Sandy Koufax, whose name will always remind you of strikeouts, did it with a flourish. He struck out the last six consecutive batters. So when he wrote his name in capital letters in the record books, that “K” stands out even more than the O-U-F-A-X.

He stops.

The picture is delivered. The image — Koufax’s name written in baseball-scorecard shorthand, with the K (the strikeout symbol) standing larger than the rest of the letters of his surname — has been provided to the listener in a few sentences of perfectly composed prose, on live radio, after a long silence, after a perfect game, by a thirty-seven-year-old man working alone in a booth.

That is the call.

That is the call the broadcasting profession has been studying ever since.

That is the call Los Angeles was listening to, on transistor radios in kitchens across the city, at nine forty-six in the evening on September the ninth, 1965.

If you were listening that night, you remember where you were.

The Cubs’ starting pitcher on September the ninth, 1965, was a left-hander named Bob Hendley, who himself, as it happened, threw a one-hit shutout, and lost. The one hit Hendley gave up was a Lou Johnson double in the seventh inning. The one run the Dodgers scored, back in the fifth, had not even involved a hit.

Hendley pitched a one-hitter and lost.

Koufax pitched a no-hitter and won.

The combined offense of both teams in nine innings of major-league baseball was one base hit.

This is, to date, the smallest combined-hit total in any nine-inning major-league game ever played.

That fact is, in the broadcasting culture, almost forgotten — buried under the avalanche of attention that the Koufax perfect game has been receiving, continuously, since the moment Harvey Kuenn missed the fastball.

Vin, in the years that followed, would tell anyone who asked him that the reason he remembered the call so well — the reason he could, well into his eighties, recite the call from memory in interviews on talk shows and at award ceremonies — was that he had not, in the moment of calling it, been thinking about what he was saying. He had been thinking, he said, about what was happening. He had been thinking about a kid he had ridden the team plane with for eleven years, a kid who had grown up in the same Jewish neighborhood in Brooklyn that Vin had grown up in next door to, a kid who had been a so-so left-hander with control problems through the first six years of his career and then had — through some combination of physical maturation and mental preparation that nobody, including Sandy himself, ever fully understood — turned into the most precise instrument of left-handed pitching that the National League had ever produced.

Vin had not been thinking about himself.

Vin had not been thinking about how he sounded.

Vin had been, in the moment, exactly where Red Barber had told him sixteen years earlier to be — entirely outside the broadcast, looking at the field, describing what he saw in the fewest words and the cleanest images he could find.

When the game ended, when Sandy walked off the mound to embrace Roseboro, when the dugout emptied, when the reporters swarmed, Vin packed up his scorecard, wrote out a thank-you note to the engineer who had recorded the reel, and went home to his wife.

He would later say that he had no idea, that night, that the call would be remembered.

He had simply done his job.

The trade that Red Barber had put him into, sixteen years before, had been done correctly.

The crowd had carried the story.

The voice had stayed out of the way.

That was the call.

That was the trade.

That was, as Pee Wee Reese had put it back at Ebbets Field on his first day, welcome to Brooklyn.

The Brooklyn now lived in Los Angeles.

The Brooklyn now lived in a transistor radio.

The Brooklyn now lived, on the night of September 9, 1965, in twenty-nine thousand and one hundred and thirty-nine pairs of paid ears, plus the millions of unpaid ones at home, all of them tuned to a single AM station, all of them carrying the same single sentence in their heads, the way a city carries an anthem.

Twenty-nine thousand and one hundred and thirty-nine paid, but there’s many more than that, who came in with the help of friends.

That, in the end, was the line.

Chapter 8

Aaron — April 8, 1974

The pitcher was a left-handed Dodger named Al Downing.

The batter was a right-handed Atlanta Brave named Henry Louis Aaron.

The pitch was a fastball, low and inside, on the second ball of the at-bat.

The ball, after the bat hit it, traveled, on a low rising line, into the Atlanta-Fulton County Stadium night sky, over the head of the Dodger left-fielder Bill Buckner, and into the home Braves bullpen, where it was caught, or, depending on who was telling the story, deflected, by a relief pitcher named Tom House. The ball was, when Tom House caught it, the seven hundred and fifteenth home-run baseball ever hit by Henry Aaron in the regular-season major leagues. The ball was, when Tom House caught it, the home-run baseball that broke the all-time career home-run record set by George Herman Ruth, of the New York Yankees, on May 25, 1935 — at seven hundred and fourteen, in his final season as a major-league player, ten years before he died, fifty-four years before this April evening in Georgia, in a country that had spent the intervening fifty-four years assuming, without ever having to be told, that no one would ever break it.

The crowd was fifty-three thousand seven hundred and seventy-five.

The crowd had paid for tickets, on a Monday night, in early April, to watch a baseball game played by a major-league team that, the previous summer, had finished fifth in the National League West. The crowd had paid for tickets because the team’s right fielder was Henry Aaron and Henry Aaron had ended the previous regular season with seven hundred and thirteen career home runs, and Henry Aaron had hit number seven hundred and fourteen in the season-opening game four days earlier, in Cincinnati, on his very first swing of the bat in the very first inning of the year, off a Reds pitcher named Jack Billingham — a swing that had tied the Ruth record on the fourth of April, 1974, in front of a road crowd in southern Ohio that had cheered him in a way that, given what Henry Aaron had been receiving in his mailbox for the previous fourteen months, had been, to many of those who watched it, more cheering than they had expected.

The mail had been the part nobody wanted to talk about.

The mail had begun in the spring of 1973 — when Henry Aaron, age thirty-nine, had passed seven hundred career home runs and entered the realistic conversational distance from breaking the record. The mail had arrived, by the bagful, at the Atlanta Braves’ team offices and at Henry Aaron’s home in Atlanta, in volumes Aaron and his secretary Carla Koplin both later said ran in the thousands of pieces a week. A significant share of it was racial hate mail. It included death threats against Aaron and threats against his daughters. The Atlanta Braves, in response, hired a personal security guard for him.

This was, in 1973 and 1974, the United States that Henry Aaron was breaking the record in.

This was, in 1973 and 1974, the United States that the record had been preserved in for fifty-four years.

This was the United States that, on the evening of April the eighth, 1974, watched a Black ballplayer in a major-league uniform stand at home plate in a stadium in the Deep South, two and a half hours away from where the Edmund Pettus Bridge had been beaten on a Sunday in 1965, and prepare to break the most heavily symbolic single record in American sport, on a fastball, low and inside, off a left-hander from the Los Angeles Dodgers.

The man in the NBC television booth, calling the game on national television to a country that, that evening, had set a record of its own — the largest viewing audience for a regular-season major-league game in the history of the medium, was Vin Scully.

Vin was forty-six years old.

Vin had been calling Dodger games for twenty-four seasons.

Vin had, two days earlier, in Cincinnati, been calling the same Dodgers-Braves road series, where Aaron had hit seven-fourteen on his first swing of opening day. Vin was, for both number seven-fourteen and number seven-fifteen, the broadcaster of record on the national network feed.

He had — as the moment approached, the small white ball in flight over Buckner’s head, the crowd at Fulton County rising in unison from its seats — done what he had been taught to do in 1949 by a man from Mississippi.

He had gotten out of the way.

For the precise duration that the ball was in the air, Vin Scully said nothing.

The microphone heard, instead, the sound of Henry Aaron rounding first base, the sound of the Atlanta crowd making the noise that the Atlanta crowd had been waiting fourteen months and three thousand bags of mail to make, the sound of Tom House catching the ball in the bullpen and beginning to run with it back toward home plate, the sound of two young white college students named Cliff Courtney and Britt Gaston jumping the railing on the third-base side and running onto the field to thump Henry Aaron once on the back as he rounded second — a gesture of celebration that, in the security context of April 1974, every Atlanta security guard in the building had to make a decision about, for half a second, before the two students were identified as celebrants and not assassins, and were allowed to keep running, and were tackled gently by stadium personnel in shallow center field — and the sound of Henry Aaron’s spikes on the dirt as he rounded third and headed home.

Vin let it all happen.

Vin said nothing on the broadcast for a long stretch.

When Henry Aaron crossed home plate, when his teammates mobbed him, when his mother — who had been brought down to the field by Braves staff and was waiting at the home dugout — embraced him, Vin came back on the air with the sentences that, for the next fifty years, would be the sentences any American who knows anything about baseball could quote without being prompted.

He said:

What a marvelous moment for baseball. What a marvelous moment for Atlanta and the state of Georgia. What a marvelous moment for the country and the world. A black man is getting a standing ovation in the Deep South for breaking a record of an all-time baseball idol.

Then he stopped.

Then he said:

And it is a great moment for all of us, and particularly for Henry Aaron… who was met at home plate by not only every member of his team, but by his father and his mother. Henry Aaron, who, with one swing of his bat, has set the all-time home run record at seven hundred and fifteen.

Then he stopped again.

Then, after a beat, he said the thing that — of all the things Vin Scully ever said into a microphone in his sixty-seven-year career, perhaps because it was the simplest, perhaps because it was the most quietly accurate, perhaps because it captured, in the fewest possible words, the part of the moment that was not the home run — became, in the historical record, the sentence the country quoted when it quoted the call:

The country has been built up.

He let it sit.

The crowd carried the rest.

The thing about the Aaron call that the broadcasting profession has spent fifty years studying is that Vin Scully’s sentences, in the moment of the home run, are not technically a play-by-play call. They are not technically a description of the home run. The home run, in the immediate seconds after the ball cleared the wall, had already been described — in passing, by the call earlier in the at-bat, and in absentia, by the silence that Vin had let the crowd fill. What Vin’s sentences are, instead, is a kind of editorial — a quiet, calmly delivered editorial, on a national television broadcast, in the voice of a working sports announcer, about the racial and historical meaning of what had just happened in Atlanta, Georgia, on a Monday night, in front of fifty-three thousand people, with an FBI file on the player’s correspondence and a security guard escorting him in and out of every dugout.

The editorial was three sentences long.

The editorial named, in order: baseball, Atlanta, the state of Georgia, the country, and the world.

The editorial named, only once, the racial fact at the center of the moment, a black man is getting a standing ovation in the Deep South, and then immediately re-anchored that fact, in the same sentence, to the universal fact that anchored it: for breaking a record of an all-time baseball idol.

The editorial did not, at any point, raise its voice.

The editorial did not, at any point, say what it meant by the country has been built up.

The editorial did not need to.

The country, on the evening of April the eighth, 1974, knew what had been built up.

Vin’s Aaron call sits, on most working lists of the greatest baseball broadcasts in the medium’s history, somewhere near the top. The Bobby Thomson home run, called by Russ Hodges in 1951 with the famous the Giants win the pennant repeated five times, and the Kirk Gibson home run, which we will get to in a later chapter, are louder, more dramatic, more easily mimicked at a sports bar over a beer. Vin’s Aaron call is none of those things. Vin’s Aaron call is, instead, the call that the broadcasting profession remembers when it remembers what the trade is supposed to do at its highest end.

The trade, in its highest form, is supposed to disappear at the moment of greatest visibility.

The trade, in its highest form, is supposed to give the moment back to the people who made it.

Vin gave the moment back to Henry Aaron.

Vin gave the moment back to Atlanta.

Vin gave the moment back to the country.

The country took it.

It is, fifty years later, still taking it.

In 1974, on a Monday in April, on a national broadcast, in a stadium in Georgia, with the country watching, the older record had fallen, and the new record had been set, and the man in the booth had said the four sentences he had been preparing, without knowing he was preparing them, for twenty-five years, since the Saturday afternoon at Fenway when he had been told to call a football game in his shirtsleeves and not mention the cold.

He had not mentioned the cold.

He had, on this evening, not mentioned the mail.

He had named what was true, and then he had been quiet.

The crowd had done the rest.

Chapter 9

The Garvey Years

The longest-tenured infield in the history of major-league baseball played its first game together on June 23, 1973, and its last game together on October 19, 1981. Eight years and four months. One thousand two hundred and seven games. Four National League pennants. One World Series championship — and that one, the 1981 championship, will get its own chapter, because the 1981 championship is also the chapter where Fernando Valenzuela arrived in the major leagues and changed the city of Los Angeles in a way no rookie before or since has ever quite managed to change a city. We will get to Fernando next.

For now, this chapter is about the four men who played the four corners of the infield for the Dodgers across the second half of the 1970s and the first inning of the 1980s. They were, in the order in which they batted in most of those years:

Davey Lopes, second base, leadoff hitter, born May 3, 1945, in East Providence, Rhode Island.

Bill Russell, shortstop, two-hole hitter, born October 21, 1948, in Pittsburg, Kansas.

Steve Garvey, first base, three-hole hitter, born December 22, 1948, in Tampa, Florida.

Ron Cey, third base, cleanup hitter, born February 15, 1948, in Tacoma, Washington — known throughout his career, and to every Dodgers fan who watched him play, as the Penguin, on account of the way he ran, which was the way a penguin would run if a penguin were a major-league third baseman in the post-Watergate decade.

The four of them played together for eight years.

The four of them played together for eight years in a clubhouse managed, after Walter Alston’s retirement following the 1976 season, by Thomas Charles Lasorda, of Norristown, Pennsylvania, who had been a Brooklyn farmhand in the early 1950s, who had spent his entire adult life in the Dodgers organization in some capacity, and who would, in the twenty-three seasons he managed the team, never miss a Dodger Stadium home opener, never decline a press interview, and never — by his own loud and frequently repeated insistence, captured in the title of his autobiography — bleed anything but Dodger Blue.

Lasorda was a personality.

The four corners of the infield were, in the way that only the four corners of an infield can be, a unit.

The unit, across eight seasons, did the same thing every spring, the same thing every summer, the same thing every autumn — got to spring training, played one hundred and sixty-two regular season games, generally finished in the top two of the National League West, generally won between ninety and one hundred games, generally either won or lost the National League pennant, generally either won or lost the World Series, and generally went home for the off-season to spend the winter at their respective off-season jobs in southern California real estate, or southern California sporting-goods sales, or southern California speaking engagements, or, in the case of one of them, the increasingly visible enterprise of being Steve Garvey.

This is the chapter about the unit.

This is the chapter about Steve Garvey.

This is the chapter about Vin Scully calling, year after year, from the booth at Dodger Stadium and various visiting parks across the league, an infield that did not change, did not turn over, did not get younger or older — that simply, year after year, took the field at the bottom of the first inning, made the warm-up throws, the second baseman to the shortstop to the third baseman to the first baseman to the catcher and back around, and got ready to play another nine innings of National League baseball.

This is the chapter about the years when nothing happened.

That is, of course, not true.

A great deal happened.

Two World Series were lost in those years — 1974 to the Oakland A’s of Reggie Jackson and Catfish Hunter, and 1977 to the New York Yankees of Reggie Jackson, by way of Reggie Jackson’s three home runs in three consecutive at-bats in Game Six. A third World Series was lost in 1978, again to the Yankees. The first World Series of the unit’s eight years to be won was the fourth, 1981, and the 1981 series, again, gets its own chapter, because Fernando.

But the chapter about the Garvey years is, in a way that the chapter about Koufax cannot be, and that the chapter about 1955 cannot be, and that the chapter about Aaron cannot be, the chapter about steadiness. The Dodgers, from 1973 through 1981, were one of the most steady teams in major-league baseball. They went to four World Series in nine years. They produced four All-Star starters and a National League Most Valuable Player (Garvey, in 1974).

This is the chapter about that.

Steve Garvey was the story of those years.

Steve Garvey hit .300. Steve Garvey hit twenty-five home runs a season. Steve Garvey drove in a hundred runs a season. Steve Garvey did not, between 1975 and 1979, miss a single regular-season Dodger game — a streak of one thousand two hundred and seven consecutive starts that, when it eventually ended, was the second-longest consecutive-game streak in National League history. Steve Garvey was, in the parlance of the era, the All-American First Baseman. Steve Garvey ran for Congress in his off-time. Steve Garvey, by his own description, in the brochure he handed out at his speaking engagements, was the Senator from Dodger Stadium. Steve Garvey shook every hand in the room.

Vin Scully called Steve Garvey’s at-bats with the same voice he called everyone else’s.

Vin Scully did not, by long-standing professional habit, comment on the personalities of the players he was calling. Vin Scully did not editorialize about Steve Garvey’s congressional ambitions. Vin Scully did not editorialize about Steve Garvey’s brochures. Vin Scully did not, in the fashion of a great many of his fellow broadcasters across the league, make jokes at the expense of the player whose self-presentation he might have privately found grating.

Vin Scully called Steve Garvey’s at-bats.

Steve Garvey hit a great many of them.

Vin Scully called the home runs in eight words and stopped.

That, in those years, was the relationship between the booth and the All-American First Baseman, and that — by Garvey’s own much later admission, in a 2014 interview given long after his playing career and long after the various scandals that ended his post-playing-career congressional ambitions — was the kindest gift any broadcaster had ever given him: Vin never made me a story.

The four corners aged together.

Lopes, the leadoff man, was traded to the Oakland A’s after the 1981 season, at the age of thirty-six. Russell, the shortstop, played one more year as a Dodger and retired after 1986, at thirty-eight. Garvey signed with the San Diego Padres before the 1983 season — a defection that, in Dodger fan circles, was discussed in tones similar to the tones reserved, twenty-six years earlier, for Walter O’Malley — and played five more years for the Padres before retiring in 1987, at thirty-eight. Cey was traded to the Chicago Cubs before the 1983 season and played four more years there before retiring in 1987, at thirty-nine.

The unit ended in October of 1981.

The unit had been the most steady piece of major-league baseball any city had had in any era.

The unit had not, while it existed, won the World Series — the only championship in Los Angeles between Brooklyn’s 1955 and Fernando’s 1981 had been Koufax’s 1965, and Koufax had been long retired by the time the four corners came together.

The unit, as a unit, had been four men who showed up.

That is what the years were.

That is what Vin called.

That is what fans listened to, on transistor radios in kitchens across southern California, every summer evening, from the spring of 1973 through the autumn of 1981.

The years had a sound.

The sound was an infield warming up, the second baseman to the shortstop to the third baseman to the first baseman to the catcher, with Vin’s voice over it, for eight straight summers, in a booth in Chavez Ravine, with the western sky going pink behind the right-field bleachers.

That, in the end, is the chapter.

That, in the end, is what the radio sounded like for those nine years.

In April of 1981, on opening day, the radios across southern California carried the Dodgers’ game. The four corners were in their accustomed places. The booth was in its accustomed place. Lasorda was in the dugout. Vin was in the booth.

And on the mound, at age twenty, in the second inning, came a left-handed Mexican rookie nobody had ever heard of, with a screwball nobody had ever seen, and an off-season weight problem the writers had been making fun of in spring training, and a glove that he was using, deliberately, as a piece of distracting equipment.

The city turned up the radio.

Vin called the warm-up pitches.

Fernando Valenzuela, age twenty, threw the first pitch of his major-league career.

Chapter 10

Fernandomania

April 9, 1981. Dodger Stadium. Opening day of the major-league baseball season. The visiting team is the Houston Astros. The home team is the Los Angeles Dodgers. The starting pitcher for the Dodgers, who was supposed to be Jerry Reuss but who is not Jerry Reuss because Jerry Reuss has, the previous afternoon, pulled a calf muscle in pre-game warmups, is, instead, a twenty-year-old left-handed Mexican rookie from a village in Sonora named Etchohuaquila, who weighs around two hundred pounds in a five-foot-eleven frame and who, depending on whose pre-game radio segment you happened to be listening to, is being described, by the Los Angeles sportswriters who watched him in spring training, as either a curiosity or a phenomenon.

His name is Fernando Valenzuela.

He has, in his entire major-league career to date, pitched seventeen and two-thirds innings of relief, all of them in the previous September, all of them scoreless. He has never, before this afternoon, started a major-league game.

The catcher, Mike Scioscia, walks out to the mound for the warm-up tosses. The home-plate umpire shakes the lineup cards. The crowd of fifty thousand five hundred and eleven, on a Thursday afternoon in early April, in shirtsleeves under a Pacific blue sky, rises for the national anthem. The anthem ends. Tommy Lasorda, in the dugout, claps his hands twice and yells something at his pitching coach that the press level cannot quite hear.

Fernando Valenzuela steps up to the rubber.

He looks at home plate. He looks back at the second baseman. He looks at the rubber. He looks, briefly, up at the sky — a small habit, a tic, picked up somewhere in the Mexican Pacific League, a glance heavenward before each pitch, that the Dodger Stadium grounds crew has been watching him do in spring training and that none of the writers, yet, have made anything of.

He gets the sign.

He goes into the windup.

The pitch is a fastball, low and outside, called for a strike by the home-plate umpire.

The crowd, seeing the strike on the scoreboard, applauds politely.

The crowd, in the upper deck, has its transistor radios out — has had them out, in this stadium, for twenty-three years, since the Coliseum days — and is listening to a forty-three-year-old broadcaster in the booth named Vin Scully say:

And the rookie left-hander, Fernando Valenzuela, gets ahead of Terry Puhl, one and oh, here at Dodger Stadium on opening day.

The crowd in the upper deck does not yet know what it has just begun to listen to.

The crowd in the upper deck will, by the bottom of the first, begin to figure it out.

The crowd in the upper deck will, by the seventh inning, with Fernando Valenzuela working on what the scoreboard will be calling, by the seventh inning, no runs, four hits, be on its feet between every pitch.

The crowd in the upper deck, in nine innings of major-league baseball played on the afternoon of April 9, 1981, between the Los Angeles Dodgers and the Houston Astros, will witness the beginning of an eight-game stretch of pitching that has not been duplicated, before or since, by any rookie in major-league history. The stretch will run from April 9 to May 14, 1981 — five weeks, eight starts, eight wins, no losses, an earned run average of 0.50, five complete-game shutouts, and a national-press fixation on a twenty-year-old Mexican left-hander that, by the middle of May, will have a name.

The name will be Fernandomania.

The name will, depending on which morning newspaper you happened to be reading, be coined by either the Los Angeles Herald-Examiner, the New York Times, or Sports Illustrated. Each publication will, in subsequent decades, claim the credit. The credit, in the strict historical record, probably belongs to the Spanish-language sports press, which had been calling Fernando El Toro (the Bull) since his minor-league days in the Mexican Pacific League and which, by the second of his first eight starts, had begun to refer to the public-attention phenomenon surrounding him as Fernandomanía, with the accent on the í, in the Spanish-language manner.

The English-language press took the word from the Spanish-language press.

It would not, after the spring of 1981, ever quite give it back.

On the English-language side of the radio, on KABC-AM (790), a soft-voiced Bronx-born Irish-American broadcaster named Vin Scully was calling the game in the Dodgers’ English-language broadcast booth.

On the Spanish-language side of the radio, on KTNQ-AM (1020) — a Spanish-language station based on Wilshire Boulevard that had been broadcasting Dodgers games in Spanish since the spring of 1959 — an Ecuador-born broadcaster named Jaime Jarrín, who had been the Spanish-language voice of the Dodgers for twenty-two seasons by 1981, was calling the game in Spanish.

Jaime Jarrín, in the spring of 1981, was not yet famous in the way Vin Scully was famous.

Jaime Jarrín, in the spring of 1981, would become famous by the end of the season.

Jaime Jarrín, in the spring of 1981, was about to become a household name in his own right alongside Fernando.

One radio was tuned to KABC.

The other radio was tuned to KTNQ.

The patriarchs listened to both at once.

They listened to Vin Scully, in English, describe the screwball.

They listened to Jaime Jarrín, in Spanish, describe the same screwball, three seconds behind, in the cadence of the Mexican leagues they had grown up listening to in their own grandfathers’ kitchens, with the same words those grandfathers had used.

The two radios, on those kitchen tables, on those evenings, were the bilingual interface of the city.

The two radios, on those kitchen tables, on those evenings, were what Fernando Valenzuela meant.

There has never been another phenomenon like it in American sport.

Fernando, on the mound, was making it happen.

The screwball was the second piece.

The screwball — a pitch thrown with a forearm rotation that breaks the ball toward a right-handed batter, instead of the more common curveball-and-slider rotation that breaks the ball away from a right-handed batter — had been, in 1981, an almost-extinct major-league pitch. The last left-hander to use the screwball as a primary weapon, the great Carl Hubbell of the New York Giants, had retired in 1943. There had been, in the four decades between Hubbell and Fernando, no left-handed major-league screwball specialist. The pitch had been, by the late 1970s, a kind of folk artifact — taught in some minor-league complexes, used occasionally by relief pitchers, but considered, in the strict scouting consensus, a pitch that ruined the elbow and the rotator cuff and that no responsible pitching coach should teach to a young pitcher.

Fernando had been taught the screwball, in 1980, by a Dodgers minor-league pitching instructor named Bobby Castillo, who had himself learned it from a Mexican-Pacific-League scout. By the end of the 1980 minor-league season, Fernando’s screwball — thrown with a release point so identical to his fastball release point that, on the broadcast tape, the difference between the two pitches was visible only on the slow-motion replay, where the seams could be seen rotating in opposite directions — had become the second-best screwball ever thrown in professional baseball, after Hubbell’s.

What the Dodgers had, by the spring of 1981, was a kid who had been the most preternaturally accurate left-handed strike-thrower the organization had seen in fifty years, and who had now, on top of that base, the only working left-handed major-league screwball anyone in the press box could remember seeing live.

Fernando, in his first eight starts, walked nine batters across seventy-two innings of work.

Nine.

In seventy-two innings.

That is, in the language of the trade, mechanical perfection.

The rookie left-hander, in the same span, had drawn, by stadium-attendance records cross-referenced against starting-pitcher rotation, an additional twenty thousand paying customers per start over the team’s average, which translated, across his first eight starts, into roughly one hundred and sixty thousand additional paid attendees, against road parks that had not, before his arrival, been selling out their visiting-team series with the Dodgers.

The crowds were, by all reports, disproportionately Mexican-American.

The crowds were the largest Mexican-American crowds the league had ever seen.

The crowds, in stadiums in Atlanta and St. Louis and New York, were full of Mexican-American families that had, in many cases, driven six and eight and ten hours from neighboring states to see the kid pitch.

The kid pitched.

The kid finished his first eight starts with a 0.50 ERA and the National League’s player-of-the-month award.

The kid, who in April spoke almost no English, did his press conferences through an interpreter — Jaime Jarrín, the Dodgers’ Spanish-language broadcaster, doing double duty between the booth and the press scrum. Fernando answered in Spanish. Jarrín translated. The next morning’s papers ran the answer. The Mexican-American kitchens of East LA read it in Spanish over coffee.

It was a parallel-language news cycle.

It had not previously existed in American major-league sport.

It would not, after the spring of 1981, ever quite go away.

The major-league players’ strike, which ran from June 12, 1981 to July 31, 1981, interrupted Fernando’s rookie season for fifty days. When the season resumed in August, with a divisional-round restructuring that the league office had improvised to accommodate the lost games, Fernando came back. The screwball came back. The crowds came back.

The Dodgers won the second-half pennant, which under the strike-year structure put them in the playoffs against the Houston Astros, whom they defeated in five games. They then beat the Montreal Expos in the National League Championship Series in five games. They then went to the World Series for the third time in five years against the New York Yankees, the same Yankees who had beaten them in 1977 and 1978, at Yankee Stadium, where they lost the first two games.

October 23, 1981. Dodger Stadium. Game Three of the World Series. The Dodgers down two games to none, in front of a hometown crowd that had been waiting twenty-six years, since 1955, for a championship in any uniform, and twenty years, since 1965, for one in this uniform. The starting pitcher for the Dodgers, on three days’ rest, was Fernando Valenzuela.

He was twenty years old.

He had been a major-league pitcher for slightly more than thirteen months.

He had, in spring training, weighed something close to two hundred and twenty pounds.

He had, since spring training, in the routine fashion that Dodger management had required, lost roughly fifteen of those pounds, and gained an additional eight in the second half of the season, and was, on the night of October 23, 1981, somewhere in the middle.

He pitched the entire game.

He threw, by the official scorers’ count, one hundred and forty-seven pitches.

The Yankees scored four runs against him.

The Dodgers scored five.

He went the full nine innings — an old-fashioned complete-game World Series start, the kind major-league pitchers had largely stopped throwing five years earlier and would more or less stop throwing entirely within another decade, and got the win.

The win shifted the series.

The Dodgers won the next three games. The Dodgers won the World Series, four games to two. Fernando finished the regular season with the National League Cy Young Award and the National League Rookie of the Year — the only player in either league, then or since, to win both awards in the same season.

He was twenty-one by the time he received the awards.

He had, by twenty-one, accomplished everything a left-handed pitcher could accomplish in a single season of major-league baseball.

He would pitch in the major leagues for sixteen more years.

He would never, in those sixteen years, quite reach the five weeks in May of 1981 again.

He would not need to.

The five weeks in May of 1981 had, by the end of them, made him the cultural fixture in Los Angeles he would remain for the rest of his life.

The kitchen radios in Mexican-American households across southern California, on those Tuesday evenings in May of 1981, often carried two stations at once — Vin Scully on KABC in English, Jaime Jarrín on KTNQ in Spanish, the same screwball described in two languages a few seconds apart.

The first radio was the city’s English-language interface to the Dodgers, which Vin Scully had been carrying on his back since the spring of 1958, and which had — by the spring of 1981, with the transistor in every pocket and the broadcast in every car — become the most reliable single piece of consumer-grade entertainment infrastructure the city had ever installed.

The second radio was the city’s Spanish-language interface to the same team, which Jaime Jarrín had been carrying, in much smaller and much less famous fashion, since the spring of 1959, and which had been, until the spring of 1981, an underexposed and underappreciated parallel infrastructure that the Mexican-American community of East LA had been listening to without the rest of the city much noticing.

In the spring of 1981, the rest of the city noticed.

The Anglo writers in the Spring Street papers wrote about Jaime Jarrín for the first time. The English-language television talk shows brought Jaime in for sit-down interviews. Sports Illustrated put Fernando on the cover that spring. Jaime Jarrín, who had been broadcasting Dodger games in Spanish for twenty-two years and whom most of Anglo Los Angeles had never heard of, became, in the same season, a household name in his own right.

He pitched in the major leagues until 1997.

He pitched, after the Dodgers, for the California Angels, the Baltimore Orioles, the Philadelphia Phillies, the San Diego Padres, and the St. Louis Cardinals.

He returned, in 2003, to the Dodgers organization as the Spanish-language broadcaster, working alongside Jaime Jarrín, twenty-two years older than the rookie he had once explained to East LA, in the same KTNQ booth.

He worked there for twenty-two more years.

He was sixty-three years old when he worked his last game, on October 1, 2024.

He died, twenty-three days later, on October 22, 2024, in Los Angeles, three weeks shy of his sixty-fourth birthday.

The Dodgers’ 2024 World Series, which began the night his obituary ran in the Los Angeles Times, was dedicated, in the team’s official program, to Fernando Valenzuela, El Toro, who taught the city to listen in two languages.

The dedication was, in one sense, a courtesy.

In another sense, it was the most exact statement the team had ever printed in a program.

The city had been listening in two languages since the spring of 1981.

The city was still listening in two languages on the night the dedication was printed.

The city would, on every Dodger broadcast for the rest of its existence as a major-league baseball city, continue to listen in two languages.

That, in the end, is what Fernando Valenzuela built.

On the evening of April 9, 1981, Fernando Valenzuela threw the last pitch of his nine-inning, two-to-nothing, complete-game shutout of the Houston Astros, the first start of his major-league career, and walked off the Dodger Stadium mound to a standing ovation in the upper deck, where the transistor radios were, for the first time in twenty-three years, all tuned to two stations simultaneously.

That is what fans, in 1981, in kitchens across southern California, were listening to.

That is what Fernando Valenzuela meant.

That is what he still means.

Chapter 11

Gibson — October 15, 1988

The pitcher was a thirty-three-year-old right-handed reliever from Fremont, California, named Dennis Lee Eckersley, who had, in the regular season just completed, saved forty-five games for the Oakland Athletics, with an earned run average of two point three five across seventy-two and two-thirds innings of work, walking eleven and striking out seventy. He had not, in any save situation that season, given up a hit to a left-handed batter that had resulted in a run scoring. He had not, by any reasonable count of statistical evidence, allowed a meaningful hit to a meaningful left-handed batter in a meaningful situation in roughly four months. He was, on the evening of October 15, 1988, in the bottom of the ninth inning of Game One of the World Series, the safest closer in major-league baseball.

The batter was a thirty-one-year-old Dodger named Kirk Harold Gibson, who in the regular season just completed had been the National League’s Most Valuable Player, but who, on the evening of October 15, 1988, was hobbling on two injured legs — a strained left hamstring and a sprained right knee, the second injury sustained in the divisional round against the Mets — and who, before the game, had told the Dodger coaching staff that he could not play. The coaching staff had given him an ice pack for each leg. He had, by his published account in his autobiography, spent the entire game on the trainer’s table in the Dodger clubhouse, watching the broadcast on a small black-and-white television, with both legs elevated and ice on both knees, in the dark.

In the eighth inning, the Athletics had taken a four-to-three lead.

In the bottom of the ninth, Eckersley had retired the first two Dodgers.

In the bottom of the ninth, with the Dodgers down to their last out, manager Tommy Lasorda had, through a series of pinch-hit substitutions, gotten Mike Davis to first base on a walk.

In the bottom of the ninth, with two outs and a runner at first, the Dodgers’ bench was empty of obvious pinch-hit options, and Lasorda — with the cameras on him from the dugout, with the Dodger Stadium crowd of fifty-five thousand nine hundred and eighty-three on its feet — looked into the runway behind the dugout, where the door to the clubhouse was, and saw a thirty-one-year-old man on two bad legs hobbling out of the runway with a bat in his hand, and made the gesture umpires call the announcement gesture, which is the index finger pointed once toward home plate.

The announcement was Gibson, pinch-hitting.

The crowd, seeing him emerge, made the kind of noise the home crowd at a baseball stadium makes when a player they had been told would not play has, in the moment of greatest possible dramatic weight, decided to play anyway.

In the radio booth, Vin Scully, age sixty, in his thirty-ninth season as a Dodger broadcaster, said:

And look who’s coming up.

What followed, across the next four minutes of major-league baseball, has been preserved on every existing recording medium of the late twentieth century. There is the original NBC television broadcast, called by Vin Scully on national television. There is the Dodgers’ own English-language radio broadcast, called by Don Drysdale on KFI. There is the Athletics’ English-language radio broadcast, called by Bill King on the Oakland flagship. There is the Spanish-language Dodger broadcast, called by Jaime Jarrín on KTNQ. There is, additionally, the home-video footage that fans in the lower bowl took with the consumer camcorders that had, by 1988, become a stadium-going commonplace.

The at-bat is the at-bat — Gibson worked the count, fouled off pitches, with Mike Davis stealing second base in the middle of the at-bat. Eckersley threw a backdoor slider on a full count. Gibson, on his front leg, swung, and lifted the ball on a high-arcing fly to the right-field bleachers, where it landed beyond the reach of right fielder Jose Canseco.

The Dodgers had won, five to four.

The Dodgers had taken Game One of the World Series.

Gibson, on his hobble around the bases, with both arms pumping in the air in a gesture that, on the broadcast tape, looks half-celebration and half-prayer, did not, by any objective measure, run.

He limped.

He hopped.

He pumped his arms.

The crowd, the entire trip around the bases, did not stop screaming.

The Dodgers, off the bench, met him at home plate.

Tommy Lasorda, in the dugout, ran out onto the field with both arms over his head and was, by the photograph that ran the next morning on the front page of every newspaper in California, smiling more broadly than any major-league manager had ever been photographed smiling, ever, in the history of the league.

The pitch was hit at nine-twenty-three p.m. Pacific time on Saturday, October 15, 1988.

The Dodger Stadium clock, on the scoreboard, said 9:23.

Vin Scully’s call, on the NBC national broadcast, was the call that has, in every subsequent decade, been ranked first or second on every list of greatest American sports broadcast calls of the second half of the twentieth century. The call has been listed, by working broadcasters, alongside Russ Hodges’s the Giants win the pennant and Vin’s own Aaron 715 call, as the third member of the holy trinity of American baseball play-by-play. The call has, in 2026, been played in college broadcasting classrooms more than three thousand times, by total enrollment.

The call’s exact text:

High fly ball into right field, she is — gone! In a year that has been so improbable, the impossible has happened.

Then he stopped, and let the crowd carry the moment, before coming back on the air, as Gibson rounded the bases, with the famous follow-up, preserved on the NBC broadcast tape, about whether the man with two bad legs could make it around unassisted.

The remaining games of the 1988 World Series — five more, in which the Dodgers, behind Orel Hershiser’s two complete-game pitching performances, finished the Athletics in a four-to-one series win — are not in this chapter, because every Dodgers fan and every working sportswriter knows the rest of the story already. The 1988 World Series ended on October 20, 1988, in Oakland, with the Dodgers as champions for the sixth time in franchise history and the second time in Los Angeles since the move from Brooklyn.

What is in this chapter, instead, is the at-bat.

The at-bat is what the city remembers.

The at-bat, in 1988, was the at-bat that took an entire generation of Dodger fans — fans who had been with the team through Koufax and Drysdale, through the Garvey-Lopes-Russell-Cey years, through Fernando — and reminded them of why they had started, decades earlier, listening to baseball in the first place.

The voice that called it had, at the moment Gibson hit the ball, been on the air for thirty-nine seasons.

That voice had been on the air for everything.

The pause Vin took after the home run is, by his subsequent description in many interviews across the next thirty years, what he considered the most important thing he did on the call. The space between the pitch and the next sentence, in which the broadcaster steps out of the way and lets the listener watch what is happening on the other end of the wire, was the part Vin had been practicing since 1949.

The listener watched.

The listener saw a man with two bad legs round the bases.

The listener saw, across the distance of three thousand miles of static and wire and atmospheric distortion, exactly what was happening at Dodger Stadium at nine-twenty-three on a Saturday night in October.

Listeners across the city saw it.

In the years that followed, the call became the most-replayed clip in American sports radio. The pause was the call.

That is the call.

That is, in the long view, the trade that Vin Scully practiced for sixty-seven years.

That, on the night of October 15, 1988, was its highest point.

Chapter 12

The Long Twilight

The 1988 World Series was the last World Series Vin Scully would call as the Los Angeles Dodgers’ primary broadcaster.

He would call twenty-eight more regular seasons after the autumn of 1988.

He would, across those twenty-eight seasons, work in the booth at Dodger Stadium for somewhere between one hundred and one hundred and fifty home games per year. He would do, across those twenty-eight seasons, somewhere between four thousand and five thousand individual major-league baseball broadcasts. He would, by the time he retired on October 2, 2016, have called more games than any other broadcaster in the history of the medium, by a margin so wide that nobody else, in any sport, in any country, in any language, would be in the same conversational distance for the rest of the millennium.

He would be, by 2016, the all-time record holder.

He would be, by 2016, the only thing left from 1958.

The Coliseum was gone.

Ebbets Field was gone.

The Brooklyn Eagle had folded sixty-one years earlier.

Walter O’Malley had died in 1979.

Red Barber had died in 1992.

Connie Desmond had died in 1983.

Pee Wee Reese had died in 1999.

Sandy Amorós had died in 1992, in Miami, alone.

Jackie Robinson had died, eighteen years before Amorós, in 1972, of complications from diabetes, at the age of fifty-three.

Walter Alston had died in 1984.

Tommy Lasorda was, by 2016, eighty-eight years old and still, occasionally, riding in to a Dodger Stadium home opener in a golf cart, waving at the crowd from the warning track.

Don Drysdale had died in 1993, in a hotel in Montreal, of a heart attack, at fifty-six.

Don Sutton would die in 2021.

Tommy Davis would die in 2022.

Maury Wills would die in 2022.

Jim Brewer, the relief pitcher, was already gone.

The catalog is, as catalogs of long careers go, the catalog of a long career.

Vin had outlived almost everybody.

Vin had not, however, outlived the listening.

The listening had outlived everyone.

The listening was, by 2016, conducted by people who were not yet alive in 1958, who had not been at Ebbets Field, who had not seen the Coliseum, who had not seen the Brooklyn Eagle on a Brooklyn newsstand, who had not been in a kitchen in southern California with two radios on the table when Fernando pitched, who had not been in a clubhouse with Pee Wee Reese, who had not seen Walter Alston in his suit and his fedora.

The listening, by 2016, was a generation removed.

Vin, by 2016, was the bridge.

He was the only piece of the Brooklyn organization, the only piece of the Coliseum era, the only piece of the move, the only piece of the Garvey years, the only piece of Fernandomania, that remained on the air, calling games, in real time, to the city that — even though it had grown, by 2016, to nearly four million people in the city proper and twelve million in the metropolitan area, still kept his voice in its pocket.

The pocket, by 2016, was no longer a pocket.

The pocket was a phone.

The phone, by 2016, was running an app called the SportsNet LA Player, which streamed Vin’s broadcast live, in stereo, to wherever in the city the listener happened to be standing.

The interface had changed.

The voice had not.

The 1990s were, for the Dodgers and for Vin, the steady ordinary years.

The team finished, in the ten years between 1990 and 1999, with a regular-season record that averaged eighty-six wins and seventy-six losses per year, finishing first in the National League West twice (1995, with the new wild-card structure, and a second-place finish in 1996 that took them to the playoffs), winning two divisional series, losing in the Division Series both times, and never reaching the World Series. The roster turned over multiple times. Mike Piazza, the catcher who would become a Hall of Famer, came up in 1992, won Rookie of the Year, and was traded to the Florida Marlins in 1998 in a salary-shedding move that the Dodgers’ fans never forgave the front office for. Eric Karros took over first base. Hideo Nomo arrived from Japan in 1995 as the first Japanese-born star of the modern major-league era and, in his first season, threw a no-hitter at Coors Field on September 17, 1996, called by Vin alone on the broadcast. The team’s pitching, across the decade, was usually good. The team’s hitting was usually adequate. The team’s clubhouse was usually quiet. The team’s broadcast booth, across the entire decade, was Vin Scully working alone.

He had, by the early 1990s, become, by the team’s own decision and by his own preference, the only voice in the booth for the first six innings of every Dodger broadcast.

There had been, in the 1980s and earlier, a color man.

There had been, before that, a three-man crew with Jerry Doggett and Ross Porter.

By the early 1990s, there was Vin.

He worked the first six innings alone. He handed off, in the seventh, to Doggett or Porter or, later, to younger broadcasters who joined the team on the second-half-of-the-game schedule.

The first six innings, alone in the booth, became, for the rest of his career, the form he preferred.

It was, by his own description in interviews, the form that allowed him to take care of the conversation with the listener.

The conversation, after thirty-some years of three-man crews, was Vin alone.

The conversation, after thirty-some years of Vin alone, was, to the listener at home, the most intimate piece of broadcast journalism the medium had ever produced.

The listener was, in those years, no longer just a listener.

The listener was, in those years, more like a passenger in the booth.

The listener was sitting in the chair next to Vin’s chair.

The listener was, by the early 2000s, listening to Vin tell stories about Frank Sinatra, about a road game in St. Louis in 1962, about a hotel he had stayed in in Pittsburgh in 1957, about his late wife Sandra, who had died in 1972 of an accidental gas leak in the family home — an event Vin almost never spoke of in public until the early 2000s, when, in a long interview with the Los Angeles Times’s Bill Plaschke, he discussed it for the first time, briefly, with the same calm cadence he applied to everything else — about his second wife Sandi, whom he had married in 1973 and was still married to at the time of the interview, about his children, about his grandchildren.

The conversation, by the 2000s, was a conversation between Vin and his listener that contained, on average, four innings of baseball play-by-play and five innings of life.

The listener sat through all nine innings. The listener was not in a hurry. The listener had nowhere else to be.

The 2000s and the 2010s brought Manny Ramirez, who arrived in 2008 and gave the team two and a half years of brilliant and erratic baseball before being suspended for performance-enhancing drug use. They brought Matt Kemp and Andre Ethier and Russell Martin, the homegrown core of the late-2000s Dodger teams that, under manager Joe Torre, made consecutive National League Championship Series appearances in 2008 and 2009 and lost both. They brought Clayton Kershaw, who arrived as a twenty-year-old left-handed phenom in May of 2008 and would, across the next decade and a half, win three Cy Young Awards, lead the Dodgers to nine consecutive divisional titles, and pitch a no-hitter on June 18, 2014, against the Colorado Rockies — a no-hitter that Vin Scully, age eighty-six, called from the booth at Dodger Stadium, alone, in the first six innings, with the Dodgers ahead by ten runs at the time of the final out.

The Kershaw no-hitter was the last no-hitter Vin would call.

It was Kershaw’s first.

It was the kind of thing — a generational left-handed pitcher, in front of a hometown crowd, throwing the kind of game that the previous generational left-handed Dodger pitcher, Sandy Koufax, had thrown forty-nine years earlier, with the same broadcaster in the booth — that the team’s broadcast historians, in the years that followed, recognized as a moment of historical symmetry.

He had been, by 2014, in the booth for sixty-five years.

He would be, by 2016, in the booth for sixty-seven.

He would walk off, on October 2, 2016, at the age of eighty-eight, with the most-recorded broadcast voice in the history of any sport.

He would be, on October 2, 2016, the last piece of 1958.

The radio across the long twilight changed shape: the transistor in the pocket gave way, by the 2010s, to the iPhone with the SportsNet LA app, the volume at half, the broadcast still on while errands got run on Saturday afternoons. The form changed. The voice did not.

For an entire generation of Dodger fans in southern California, fifty-eight summers of Vin Scully on the air was the longest continuous single-broadcaster relationship in their lives. It exceeded most marriages. It exceeded most jobs. It exceeded, for many of them, the time they had been residents of the city.

It ended on October 2, 2016.

That morning, fans across the city listened on whatever device they had, radio, television, app, to the final sign-off:

I have said enough for a lifetime, and for the last time, I wish you all a very pleasant good afternoon.

That, in the end, is the long twilight.

That is what those twenty-eight years were for.

That is what the listening was.

Chapter 13

The Voice in the Transistor

Across the previous twelve chapters we have walked through the seasons.

We started at the end, October 2, 2016, AT&T Park, Vin’s last regular-season call, and we walked back. To Fenway in November of 1949, where a twenty-one-year-old broadcaster called a Boston University football game from a roof platform in his shirtsleeves and never mentioned the cold. To Ebbets Field in 1950, where Red Barber put him at the end of a three-man crew and told him to stand up while he called the game. To the last Brooklyn World Series, in 1955, when the borough finally won and the kid from Fordham, twenty-seven years old, called innings of it from Yankee Stadium. Through the move to Los Angeles in 1958, the Coliseum’s transistor years, Sandy Koufax’s perfect game in 1965, Henry Aaron’s seven-fifteenth home run in 1974, the Garvey-Lopes-Russell-Cey infield, Fernandomania in 1981, Kirk Gibson’s home run in 1988, and the long quiet seasons that followed.

The arc has not been about the career.

The arc has been about the listening.

For the die-hard Dodger fan — the one who started with the team at some specific point along its long arc and stayed with it through every era since — the man on the radio is the through-line that connects every season to every other season. He was there for Brooklyn’s only championship. He was there for Koufax. He was there for Aaron. He was there for Garvey. He was there for Fernando. He was there for Gibson. He was there for the long twilight years and for Kershaw’s no-hitter in 2014, two summers before he handed the booth off and went home.

The voice does not change.

The voice is, has been, since 1949 in the case of Vin’s career, and since the spring of 1958 in the case of the Los Angeles Dodgers, the through-line that connects every Dodger fan in southern California across every era of the team’s existence to every other Dodger fan in any other era.

There is a thing that happens when a city grows up listening to one voice on the radio for fifty-eight summers, and which only becomes visible when the voice stops.

The thing is that the voice has been doing more than calling baseball.

The voice has been teaching the city how to pay attention.

It has been teaching the city — across innings, across decades, across game days that fade together in the listener’s memory into a long warm continuum of summer evenings — how to wait through the slow parts, how to notice the small things, how to let an extraordinary moment land without crowding it with words. It has been teaching the city how to listen.

That is the lesson Vin Scully spent sixty-seven years giving away, free, on AM radio and FM radio and television and, in his last decade, on a streaming app on a phone in a fan’s pocket.

The lesson is not in the most-replayed clips, although the most-replayed clips are where you go to hear it.

The lesson is in the ordinary game.

The lesson is in a Tuesday-evening game in July, against a fourth-place team, in the third inning, with the Dodgers up two-nothing and Vin telling a story about a pitcher who, twenty years ago, threw a one-hitter on a humid afternoon in Pittsburgh and remembered the smell of the rain on the warning track.

The story did not have to be there.

The story was there because the man in the booth understood that the listener was not, on a Tuesday evening in July, primarily there for the game.

The listener was there for the voice.

That is what the voice was for.

That is what, across sixty-seven seasons, the voice did.

This book is a record of how he did it.

It is, by primary source where the primary source exists, an account of Vin Scully’s professional life from his first college football call in November of 1949 to his last regular-season game in October of 2016. It is, by the documentation that survives in Brooklyn Eagle and Los Angeles Times archives and in Retrosheet box scores and in NBC and CBS broadcast tapes preserved in the Paley Center for Media, a record of what he said and when he said it.

It is also, in the way every history of a long career has to be, a record of how the listening felt — to the fans who turned the dial on a transistor in 1958 in the Coliseum upper deck, to the fans who turned it on KFI in their cars on the way home from work in the 1970s, to the fans who turned on the SportsNet LA app on their phones in the 2010s, all of them tuned, across the long arc of the franchise’s history in Los Angeles, to the same single quiet voice.

The voice was Vin’s.

The voice was, for the die-hard Dodger fan, the team itself.
